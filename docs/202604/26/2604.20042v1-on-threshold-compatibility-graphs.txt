Abstract 

Pairwise Compatibility Graphs (PCGs ) form a tree-metric graph class that originated in phylogeny and has since attracted sustained interest in graph theory. Several natural generalizations have been proposed in order to overcome the expressive limitations of classical PCGs , including 

k-interval-PCGs , k-OR-PCGs , and k-AND-PCGs . In this paper, we introduce (k, t )-threshold-PCGs , a threshold-based framework that unifies these generalized notions: adjacency is determined by whether at least t among k underlying PCG predicates accept the vertex pair. We investigate the expressive power of this model from both constructive and asymptotic viewpoints. On the positive side, we show that every graph on n vertices is a (n, t )-threshold-PCG for every 1 ≤ t ≤ n. On the negative side, we prove that for every fixed pair (k, t ), the class of (k, t )-threshold-PCGs is asymptotically rare among all graphs. As a consequence, we obtain sharp separations from previously studied models, including a strict expressive gap relative to k-interval-PCGs . We also study explicit obstruction families through incidence graphs and derive additional structural consequences for the conjunction case, including the strictness of the k-AND-PCG hierarchy and the failure of closure under complement.

## 1 Introduction 

Pairwise Compatibility Graphs (PCGs ) were introduced by Kearney, Munro, and Phillips in the context of phylogeny, where compatibility relations among taxa are modeled through distances in an underlying edge-weighted tree [ 21 ]. Over time, however, the notion has developed into a graph-theoretic representation model of independent interest. Informally, a graph is a PCG if its vertices can be associated with the leaves of an edge-weighted tree so that two vertices are adjacent exactly when their leaf-to-leaf distance lies in a prescribed interval. This definition is easy to state and geometrically appealing, but the class it defines has proved to be surprisingly subtle. A large body of work has been devoted to understanding the expressive power of PCGs .On the positive side, several nontrivial graph classes are known to admit PCG representa-tions; for example, graphs of Dilworth number at most two are PCGs [ 9, 10 ], triangle-free outerplanar 3-graphs are PCGs [ 25 ], and further sufficient conditions are known for broader families [ 12 , 20 ]. On the negative side, the class is not universal: it was shown that not every graph is a PCG [29 ], and subsequent work developed explicit obstruction families, proof techniques for non-PCG ness, and enumerative studies of minimal non-PCG graphs [15 , 4, 5 , 3]. Surveys such as [ 24 , 13 , 14 ] document both the breadth of the area and the persistence of several basic open problems. Even at small orders, the boundary is delicate: all graphs on at most seven vertices are PCGs [6], while more intricate behavior appears immediately beyond that range.

These limitations have motivated a number of natural generalizations. One direction relaxes the single-interval requirement while retaining a common witness tree, leading to 

k-interval-PCG [ 1, 19 ]. Another direction combines several PCG representations on the same vertex set by Boolean operations, giving rise to classes such as k-OR-PCG and 

k-AND-PCG [8, 14 ]. These models enlarge the expressive scope of classical PCGs , but they have largely been studied in parallel rather than within a single framework. As a consequence, some basic comparative questions have remained poorly understood. For example, the recent literature asks for sharper separations between multi-interval and Boolean-combination models, for explicit obstruction families in low-level OR /AND classes, and for a better understanding of distinguished hard families from the multi-interval literature [8, 14]. In this paper, we unify these directions through a threshold-based framework. Given 

k underlying PCG predicates, we declare a pair of vertices adjacent whenever at least t

of them accept the pair. This yields the class of (k, t )-threshold-PCG s. The definition subsumes several previously studied models as special cases: ordinary PCGs correspond to (1 , 1) , k-OR-PCG is recovered when t = 1 , and k-AND-PCG is recovered when t = k.In this sense, (k, t )-threshold-PCG s provide a single framework for studying fixed-size combinations of tree-distance graph representations. This framework leads to a clear dichotomy. If the number of constituent predicates is allowed to grow with the graph size, then the model becomes universal: every graph on 

n vertices is a (n, t )-threshold-PCG for every 1 ≤ t ≤ n. In contrast, if the number of predicates is fixed, then even after allowing all thresholds, the resulting classes occupy only an asymptotically vanishing fraction of all graphs. Thus the threshold model is both expressive and structurally restrictive. Moreover, it provides a convenient setting in which several phenomena can be studied uniformly: counting arguments yield separations from multi-interval representations, incidence graphs give explicit obstruction families, and the conjunction case exhibits both a strict hierarchy and failure of complement closure. 

Our contributions 

This paper initiates the systematic study of (k, t )-threshold-PCG s. Our main contributions are as follows. We prove a universality theorem: every graph on n vertices is a (n, t )-threshold-PCG 

for every 1 ≤ t ≤ n.We prove an asymptotic sparsity theorem: for every fixed integer k, even after allowing all thresholds t ∈ [k], the union of the corresponding threshold classes has asymptotic density zero among all graphs. We compare threshold combinations with multi-interval representations and show that 

(k, t )-threshold-PCG s can be substantially more expressive. In particular, if f (k) ∈

o(k2/ log k), then for all sufficiently large k there exists a graph that is a (k, t )-threshold-PCG but not an f (k)-interval-PCG . As a consequence, for all sufficiently large k,

k-interval-PCG ⊊ k-OR-PCG .

We develop explicit obstruction families via set-system incidence graphs. This yields concrete lower bounds for threshold representations, explicit infinite families outside low-level threshold classes, and quantitative bounds for some graph classes. In the conjunction setting, we prove that the hierarchy of k-AND-PCG classes is strict:

We also show that the classes k-AND -PCG and k -interval-PCG are not closed under complement. Taken together, these results show that threshold combinations provide a robust and mean-ingful extension of the PCG paradigm. They are expressive enough to unify and strengthen several known variants, yet rigid enough to admit strong counting theorems, explicit obstruc-tions, and nontrivial hierarchy results. We believe this makes (k, t )-threshold-PCG s a natural object of study in their own right and a useful organizing framework for future work on tree-distance graph representations.

## 3 Universality When the Number of Predicates Grows 

We now show that the threshold model becomes universal once the number of underlying 

PCG predicates is allowed to grow with the graph size. We first record two simple padding lemmas. Since both Kn and Kn are trivially PCGs ,they may be used as auxiliary predicates in threshold constructions. 

▶ Lemma 7. Let G be a graph on n vertices. If G is a q-OR-PCG , then G is a (n, t )-threshold-PCG for every 1 ≤ t ≤ n − q + 1 .

Proof. Since G is a q-OR-PCG , write G = G1 ∪ · · · ∪ Gq , where each Gi is a PCG . Add 

t − 1 copies of Kn and n − q − (t − 1) copies of Kn. Then every edge of G is accepted by at least one of G1, . . . , G q and by all t − 1 copies of Kn, hence by at least t predicates in total. Every nonedge is accepted by none of G1, . . . , G q and only by the t − 1 copies of Kn, hence by exactly t − 1 predicates. Therefore, G is a (n, t )-threshold-PCG . ◀▶ Lemma 8. Let G be a graph on n vertices. If G is a q-AND-PCG , then G is a (n, t )-threshold-PCG for every q ≤ t ≤ n. 

Proof. Since G is a q-AND-PCG , write G = G1 ∩ · · · ∩ Gq , where each Gi is a PCG . Add 

t − q copies of Kn and n − t copies of Kn. Then every edge of G is accepted by all q original predicates and by all t − q copies of Kn, hence by at least t predicates. Every nonedge is missing from at least one of G1, . . . , G q , so it is accepted by at most (q − 1) + ( t − q) = t − 1

predicates. Therefore, G is a (n, t )-threshold-PCG . ◀▶ Theorem 9. For every integer n ≥ 1 and every t ∈ [n], every graph on n vertices is a

## 6 Set-system Incidence Graphs as Explicit Obstructions 

The counting arguments of the previous sections show that fixed threshold classes are asymptotically sparse, but they do not identify concrete obstructions. In the literature on 

PCG variants, this distinction is important. The Ramsey-type arguments of [ 8] imply that, for every fixed integer r, there exist graphs outside r-OR-PCG and outside r-AND-PCG ,but those arguments are existential and do not produce explicit examples. The same paper and the recent survey [ 14 ] highlight the need for sharper obstruction families, especially for small values of r. In particular, [ 8] asks for the smallest graph that is not a 2-OR-PCG 

(and similarly for 2-AND-PCG and 2-interval-PCG ). In this section we address this issue by studying bipartite incidence graphs of set systems. ## 8 Conclusions and Open Problems 

We introduced (k, t )-threshold-PCG s as a common framework for several existing general-izations of PCGs . This viewpoint simultaneously recovers ordinary PCGs , k-OR-PCG s, and k-AND-PCG s, while remaining close to the original tree-distance model. Our results exhibit a clear dichotomy. When the number of constituent predicates grows with the graph size, the model becomes universal: every graph on n vertices is a 

(n, t )-threshold-PCG for every t ∈ [n]. In contrast, for fixed k, even after allowing all thresholds t ∈ [k], the resulting classes have asymptotic density zero. We also showed that threshold combinations can be much more expressive than multi-interval representations: if f (k) ∈ o(k2/ log k), then for all sufficiently large k there exists a graph that is a (k, t )-threshold-PCG but not an f (k)-interval-PCG . In particular, for all sufficiently large k,

k-interval-PCG ⊊ k-OR-PCG .

Beyond counting arguments, we developed explicit obstruction families via set-system incidence graphs. These yield concrete lower bounds for threshold representations and, in particular, explicit infinite families outside low-level threshold classes. The same approach also gives strong lower bounds for the family Hm in the multi-interval setting. Finally, for conjunctions, we proved that k-AND-PCG ⊊ (k + 1) -AND-PCG for every k ≥ 1, and that 

k-AND -PCG is not closed under complement. The main remaining questions are quantitative. Our results leave a substantial gap between lower and upper bounds for explicit obstruction families, and they also leave open the small-parameter behavior of several threshold and multi-interval classes.
