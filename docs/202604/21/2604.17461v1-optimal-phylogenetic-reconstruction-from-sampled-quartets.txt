Abstract 

Quartet Reconstruction, the task of recovering a single phylogenetic tree from smaller trees on four species called quartets , is a well-studied problem in theoretical computer science with far-reaching connections to statistics, graph theory and biology. Given a random sample containing 

m noisy quartets, labeled according to an unknown ground-truth tree T on n taxa, we want to learn the tree structure of T with small generalization error, i.e., to output a tree bT that is 

close to T in terms of quartet distance and can predict the classification of unseen quartets. Unfortunately, the empirical risk minimizer corresponds to the NP -hard problem of finding a tree that maximizes agreements with the sampled quartets, and earlier works in approximation algorithms gave (1 − ϵ)-approximation schemes (PTAS) for dense instances with m = Θ( n4)

quartets, or for m = Θ( n2 log n) quartets randomly sampled from T .Prior to our work, it was unknown how many samples are information-theoretically required to learn the tree, and whether there is an efficient reconstruction algorithm. We present optimal results for reconstructing an unknown phylogenetic tree T from a random sample of m = Θ( n)

quartets, potentially corrupted under the standard Random Classification Noise (RCN) model. This matches the Ω( n) lower bound required for any meaningful tree reconstruction, as for 

m = o(n), large parts of T cannot be recovered, and exact tree reconstruction ( ϵ = 0 ) requires 

Ω( n3) quartets. Our contribution is twofold: first, we give a tree reconstruction algorithm that, not only achieves a (1 − ϵ)-approximation for Quartet Reconstruction, but most importantly 

recovers a tree close to T in quartet distance; second, we show a new Θ( n) bound on the Natarajan dimension of phylogenies (an analog of VC dimension in multiclass classification), which may be of independent interest. Coupled together, these imply that our reconstructed tree 

bT will generalize well to unseen quartets. Our analysis relies on a new Quartet-based Embedding and Detection ( QED ) procedure, that repeatedly identifies and removes well-clustered subtrees from the (unknown) ground-truth T via semidefinite programming.

How can we reconstruct a phylogenetic tree from partial information on how different species are related? This is a classic question in computational biology and a central topic in the field of Phy-logenetics, where a major task is to recover the Tree of Life for living and extinct species (Colonius and Schulze, 1981; Maddison et al., 2007; Jansson et al., 2016). Beyond biology, similar questions for finding hierarchical tree representations of data from labeled samples (often called a hierar-chical clustering ) naturally arise across disciplines, ranging from linguistics with the evolution of languages (Gray et al., 2009; Nakhleh et al., 2005; Walker et al., 2012), to computer science and statistics, as datasets and networks tend to organize hierarchically (Ward Jr, 1963; Ravasz and Barabási, 2003; Clauset et al., 2008; Nickel and Kiela, 2017). A common approach for reconstructing a tree works by first obtaining small subtrees on over-lapping sets of taxa, most commonly on 4 species called quartets , and then, trying to aggre-gate/amalgamate these subtrees into one big tree over the whole set (Strimmer and Von Haeseler, 1996; Jansson et al., 2005; Byrka et al., 2010; Jansson et al., 2016). One of the most basic problems here is the NP -complete problem of Quartet Reconstruction (Steel, 1992; Jiang et al., 1998, 2001): 

Problem 1.1 (Quartet Reconstruction, MaxQuartetRec ). Given a set L of n items, and a sample of m quartets of the form ab |cd with a, b, c, d ∈ L, Quartet Reconstruction aims to find an unrooted tree T with n leaves labeled from L, so that the path a, b is vertex-disjoint from the path 

c, d in T , for all given quartets (in this case we say quartet ab |cd is satisfied). The corresponding optimization problem of maximizing satisfied quartets is denoted MaxQuartetRec .

Quartet-based reconstruction has played a central role in phylogenetics, both in theory and in practice, see survey by Chor (1998) and standard textbooks by Semple and Steel (2003); Felsenstein (2004). A quartet tree, i.e., a phylogenetic tree 1 on 4 elements a, b, c, d , constitutes the basic infor-mative building unit in phylogenetic applications (Avni et al., 2015; Avni and Snir, 2019), and lies at the heart of popular quartet-puzzling methods (Strimmer and Von Haeseler, 1996; Yang et al., 2013; Reaz et al., 2014). Recently, assuming Unique Games (Khot, 2002), it was shown that MaxQuarte-tRec is NP -hard to approximate beyond a 13 -approximation factor (Chatziafratis and Makarychev, 2023). Note that there are only 3 distinct quartets on a, b, c, d , namely ab |cd, ac |bd, ad |bc , so a  

> 13

-approximation is also obtained by the trivial algorithm that outputs a random tree on n leaves (each quartet is equally likely), which means that MaxQuartetRec is approximation resistant in the worst-case (Håstad, 2001; Hast, 2005; Guruswami et al., 2011).

Reconstruction of an unknown tree T . In most applications, indeed there is a meaningful ground-truth tree T to be recovered, often called a latent tree (Anandkumar et al., 2011; Kandiros et al., 2023), and the m given quartets are obtained by some noisy classification on a random sample (a, b, c, d ) at the leaves (see App. F, Fig. 5), e.g., by an experiment on the genomes of 4 species, or by asking an expert (Wu et al., 2008; Vikram and Dasgupta, 2016; Emamjomeh-Zadeh and Kempe, 2018; Ghoshdastidar et al., 2019). Quartets offer several advantages, as they do not make assumptions on numerical values of pairwise distances (see Chapter 12 in Felsenstein (2004)), or on the ancestry of species from triplets (Aho et al., 1981; Byrka et al., 2010; Bryant et al., 2000; Wu et al., 2008; Emamjomeh-Zadeh and Kempe, 2018). In fact, in statistics, quartets played a major role in the resolution of Steel’s conjecture for the Cavender-Farris-Neyman/Ising mutation model: Mossel (2004); Daskalakis et al. (2006, 2011) designed “quartet tests” that avoided the bias of sampled sequences of characters at the leaves of a ground-truth tree, obtaining the optimal reconstruction bounds for the mutation probability. Finally, earlier works study PAC-learning of evolutionary trees under various stochastic models for evolution, such as the two-state general Markov model (Ambainis et al., 1997; Farach and Kannan, 1999; Cryan et al., 2001). Here, our focus is on reconstructing a tree bT that is close to T , based on m noisy quartet samples from T . (2017). In this paper, we develop algorithms for recovery in quartet distance from noisy quartets. 

Random Classification Noise (RCN) Model. Let T be the class of all phylogenetic trees on the n leaves labeled from L. Moreover, let T ∈ T be any ground-truth tree whose structure we want to recover via a tree bT , such that dist( bT , T ) ≤ ϵ, for some target accuracy ϵ. Notice that being close in quartet distance means that such a tree bT generalizes well, i.e., it would be successful even 2at predicting unseen quartets. Towards this, we follow the standard Random Classification Noise (RCN) model under the uniform distribution (Angluin and Laird, 1988; Natarajan et al., 2013), where we are given a random sample of m quartets, each of which is generated as follows: 

• Draw 4 distinct elements (a, b, c, d ) uniformly at random from the set L of leaves; 

• With probability 1 − η, we get the correct quartet label 

T (a, b, c, d ) ∈ {{ ab |cd }, {ac |bd }, {ad |bc }} ;

• Otherwise, w.p. η, we get a random wrong quartet label, that disagrees with T (a, b, c, d ).A quartet t = ab |cd drawn from the RCN model with error η is denoted as t ∼ RCN (η) (η, or an upper bound to it, is known to the algorithm). Notice that since there are 3 labels for a quartet, 

η = 23 corresponds to a uniformly random quartet, so from now on η < 23 . The RCN noise model is quite standard as it captures for instance the aforementioned scenarios where labeled examples are provided by a noisy expert or experiment. The criterion for successfully learning the ground-truth 

T is generalization :

Problem 1.3 (Phylogenetic Tree Learning) . Let T be the class of all phylogenetic trees on n leaves. Given small constants ϵ, δ > 0, using as few sampled quartets as possible, efficiently find a tree 

bT ∈ T , that generalizes well to unseen quartets. That is, with probability 1 − δ, find a tree bT ∈ T so the probability of error on 4 random elements (a, b, c, d ) is small compared to the best tree T ∈ T ,or equivalently, 2,

Pr[ bT (a, b, c, d )̸ = T (a, b, c, d )] ≤ ϵ

where T (a, b, c, d ) ∈ {{ ab |cd }, {ac |bd }, {ad |bc }} denotes the unique quartet satisfied by tree T ∈ T .

Observe that this is the standard generalization framework of PAC-learning (Shalev-Shwartz and Ben-David, 2014); here our algorithms will be robust, so we also allow input samples to be noisy, and the reconstructed output tree bT will still have small quartet distance from the ground-truth 

T , dist( bT , T ) ≤ ϵ. MaxQuartetRec (Problem 1.1) corresponds to the Empirical Risk Minimizer (ERM) on the sample of size m; indeed, approximating the MaxQuartetRec objective can be seen as a prerequisite step towards recovery and it has been extensively studied in theoretical computer science (Jiang et al., 1998, 2001; Byrka et al., 2010; Snir and Yuster, 2011, 2012). However, learning the structure of T is more challenging and the main question behind our work is the following: 

Main Question (Sampling Complexity and Efficient Tree Learning) . How many (noisy) quartet samples m are necessary to learn the underlying ground-truth tree T ? Most importantly, is there a polynomial-time algorithm that reconstructs T (up to generalization error ϵ)?

3Sampling Complexity. In PAC-learning, the Natarajan dimension (Natarajan, 1989; Haussler and Long, 1995) captures the sampling complexity for learning a set of functions, generalizing the VC-dimension from binary to multi-class settings (Shalev-Shwartz and Ben-David, 2014; Daniely et al., 2015; Avdiukhin et al., 2023). Natarajan dimension is suitable for our tree learning purposes, since 4 elements a, b, c, d can be classified in three different ways by a ground-truth tree T , namely 

T (a, b, c, d ) ∈ {{ ab |cd }, {ac |bd }, {ad |bc }} , but we defer precise definitions of Natarajan shattering to Section 5. Our first result is a tight bound on the Natarajan dimension of learning trees (information-theoretically) from quartets: 

Theorem 1.4 (Sampling Complexity of Tree Learning) . The Natarajan dimension of learn-ing phylogenetic trees on n leaves from quartets is Θ( n), hence the sampling complexity of Problem 1.3 is m = Θ ϵ,δ (n) quartets. 

This means that, in principle, the reconstruction of the ground-truth tree T is possible with only m = Θ( n) samples. Notice here, this result holds for any distribution, not necessarily the uniform. However, previous results on efficient PTAS algorithms required much larger samples of size m = Θ( n4), later improved to m = Θ( n2 log n) (Jiang et al., 1998, 2001; Snir and Yuster, 2011, 2012). Observe that since there are |T | ≤ (2 n − 2) 2n−4 leaf-labeled trees on n leaves, a direct upper bound for the Natarajan dimension is O(n log n). To improve the bound, we do a quartet-preserving embedding of a phylogenetic tree T , by associating each quadruple vi, v j , v k, v l of leaves to a degree-8

polynomial. The sign of the polynomial reveals what the correct split for vi, v j , v k, v l is according to T , and then we bound the number of sign-patterns of polynomials (Warren, 1968; Alon et al., 1985). Up to constant factors, our result also recovers the bound in Avdiukhin et al. (2023) for the rooted triplet consistency problem, which is solvable in polynomial time (Aho et al., 1981; Byrka et al., 2010; Emamjomeh-Zadeh and Kempe, 2018). Unfortunately, contrary to triplets, checking satisfiability of quartets is NP -complete (Steel, 1992). 

Algorithm for Tree Learning. Even in the absence of noise ( η = 0 ), a trivial lower bound for any meaningful tree reconstruction to be possible in terms of quartet distance is m = Ω( n), as otherwise, with m = o(n), most of the ground-truth tree would not even be observed in the sample. Our main result is a polynomial-time algorithm that recovers an unknown ground-truth hierarchy T

from m = Θ( n) quartet samples on its leaves L, matching the above information-theoretic bound, and that is robust against noise from RCN (η) model above. Our main technical ingredient is a tree reconstruction algorithm that we call Quartet-based Embedding and Detection (QED ) that repeatedly detects well-clustered subtrees of T via semidefinite programming, then removes them, and ultimately combines them into a final tree bT with small quartet distance from T . In particular, our algorithm implies a new PTAS for MaxQuartetRec with m = Θ( n) noisy quartets improving the previously-best sample size of m = Θ( n2 log n) (Jiang et al., 1998, 2001; Snir and Yuster, 2011; Snir and Rao, 2012). 4Theorem 1.5 (Tree Learning with m = Θ( n) quartets) . 1.3 Further Related Work 

Quartets have played an important role in phylogenomics, statistics and machine learning for re-covering latent tree structures (Daskalakis et al., 2006, 2011; Anandkumar et al., 2011; Avni et al., 2015; Avni and Snir, 2019; Kandiros et al., 2023), and also in logic and algebra due to closure operations on trees (Grünewald et al., 2007; Bodirsky and Mueller, 2010; Bodirsky, 2012; Bodirsky et al., 2017). More technically distant works on recovering trees from pairwise information on the leaves have also been widely-studied in computer science and metric embeddings. For example, a related problem arising in phylogenetics is given an n × n matrix describing dissimilarity on n

taxa, find the tree metric or ultrametric that best fits the matrix (Agarwala et al., 1998; Ailon and Charikar, 2005; Charikar and Gao, 2024; Cohen-Addad et al., 2024, 2025). Algorithms for commu-nity detection in the Stochastic Block Model have been extensively studied in the literature; see e.g., Boppana (1987); Feige and Kilian (2001); McSherry (2001); Coja-Oghlan (2010); Massoulié (2014); Makarychev et al. (2012, 2014b, 2016); Mossel et al. (2015); Feldman et al. (2015); Gué-5don and Vershynin (2016); Abbe (2018). For hierarchical clustering, these settings were studied by Cohen-Addad et al. (2017, 2019); Manghiuc and Sun (2021) in beyond-worst-case models with a planted tree, where they show a constant-factor approximation for the similarity-based objective introduced by Dasgupta (2016). These works differ from our setting as they do not address quartets.

# 2 Technical Overview 

In this section, we provide an overview of the tree reconstruction algorithm. We assume that there exists a ground-truth tree T with n leaves. The algorithm receives a collection of m = O(n) random quartets (ab |cd ). Each quartet is obtained by sampling four distinct leaves a, b, c, d uniformly at random from the set of all 4-tuples of leaves for which the relation (ab |cd ) holds in the ground-truth tree T . To model noise, each quartet is independently replaced with one of the two unsatisfied quartets, (ac |bd ) or (ad |bc ), each with probability η/ 2 (see Section 1.1 for details). Thus, η represents the probability that a sampled quartet is misclassified, and η = 0 corresponds to the noiseless setting. Our goal is to reconstruct the underlying ground-truth tree T from this collection of (possibly noisy) quartet constraints. Given such samples, we aim to recover a tree bT whose structure is close to T in the quartet distance. We show that, with high probability, the reconstructed tree bT differs from T on at most an ϵ-fraction of quartets while using only polynomial time and a number of samples linear in n (see Theorem 1.5). In this overview, we focus on the noiseless classification setting, where η = 0 . Note that even when η = 0 , we cannot directly use an algorithm for finding a satisfying assignment, since this problem is NP -complete and hence no polynomial-time algorithm exists (unless P = NP ). Later, we will build on the ideas presented in this section when describing the algorithm that applies to the case η ∈ [0 , 23 ).The ground-truth tree T is initially unrooted. For convenience, we introduce a root r by sub-dividing one of the edges of T so that the two subtrees adjacent to r contain between one-third and two-thirds of the leaves; that is, their sizes differ by at most a factor of 2:1 . From this point onward, we view T as a rooted tree. Note that the quartet relation (ab |cd ) is independent of the choice of the root: it evaluates to true if and only if the paths between a and b and between c and 

d are vertex-disjoint. The starting point of our discussion is the following observation: the probability that, for a given pair of vertices (a, b ), there exists a quartet constraint (ab |cd ) or (cd |ab ) in the random sample is slightly higher when the vertices a and b are close to each other rather than far apart. Nevertheless, we can use the graph G to extract information about the structure of the under-lying tree. Building on techniques for learning communities in the Stochastic Block Model with 

k communities and adversarial noise (Makarychev, Makarychev, and Vijayaraghavan, 2016), we develop a procedure that identifies a set of leaves belonging to a subtree of size δn , where δ is a sufficiently small constant. The idea behind this learning step is as follows. Let u be a vertex in the tree, and let Lu denote the set of leaves in the subtree rooted at u. Suppose |Lu| ≈ δn . Then, the probability that two leaves a, b ∈ Lu are connected by an edge in G is slightly higher than the probability of an edge ac ,where a ∈ Lu and c / ∈ Lu. However, note that (i) for leaves c that are close to Lu, the probability of the edge ac may be very close to that of ab ; and (ii) for leaves c that are very far from a, this probability can actually be quite large. Thus, we cannot identify the set Lu exactly but can only recover it approximately: we find a set Ru such that 

|Lu△Ru| ≤ O(δ2n).

Towards identifying such a set Ru, we employ a semidefinite program (SDP), specified in Eqs. (10)-(13), as a community detection procedure. This SDP embeds the graph into a Euclidean space, with the hope that the vectors corresponding to vertices within S form a cluster – that is, they are tightly grouped together and well separated from the remaining vertices. However, as discussed above, the probability that an edge between two vertices u, v ∈ S is present is nearly identical to the probability of an edge between u ∈ S and a vertex w / ∈ S when w is sufficiently close to u. Moreover, these probabilities may depend on the unknown topology of the underlying tree T . To account for this, we introduce a buffer set B surrounding S, consisting of intermediate vertices that separate S from the remainder of the ground-truth tree T . Our analysis shows that 

|B| ≤ O(δ2n). Finally, to ensure that the SDP does not return an excessively large set that could deviate from the intended tree structure of T , we include an appropriate spreading constraint (see Eq. # 3 Tree Reconstruction 

In this section, we present the Quartet-based Embedding and Detection (QED) tree reconstruction algorithm and prove Theorem 1.5. The construction follows the technical overview provided in Section 2. Recall that we assume the ground-truth tree T is rooted, and that both its left and right subtrees contain at least one third of all leaves. The main building block of the algorithm is the community detection procedure. In Section 4.4 we describe a procedure to generate a graph based on the quartet set Q and introduce the notion of a good graph G, defined as a graph that satisfies the conditions of Lemma 4.2. Lemma 4.2 shows that, with probability exponentially close to 1 (specifically, with probability at least 1 −

poly(1 /δ ) exp( −1.5n)) over the randomness in the quartet set and the internal randomness of the algorithm, the graph constructed is good. We now present the SBM-inspired algorithm that recovers the leaves corresponding to a subtree Tu

of the tree T . At a high level, the algorithm iteratively recovers such sets to reconstruct a tree close to the ground truth. To achieve this, it constructs a graph G′(L′, E ′) using the quartet dataset, 

L′ here corresponds to the set of active leaves. The algorithm then uses a semidefinite program to 18 approximately recover the subset of L′ that are leaves of that subtree. The size of the recovered subtree is approximately δn , where δ is a small constant dependent on the accuracy parameter ε.Our algorithm recovers the intended set up to an error of O(δ2n). # 5 Natarajan Dimension of Quartets 

Here we derive the bound on the Natarajan dimension Nquart (n) for learning phylogenetic trees from quartets. Recall, that the Natarajan dimension governs learnability for multiclass settings, similar to how VC dimension governs learnability in binary classification (see also the Multiclass Fundamental Theorem 29.3, Chapter 29 in (Shalev-Shwartz and Ben-David, 2014)). Since our Theorem 5.7 here guarantees that Nquart (n) ≤ 10 n, this means that a linear in n sample of quartets suffices for generalization, and so our previous tree reconstruction algorithm finds a tree bT

with low generalization error. We now formally define the Natarajan dimension for learning quartets. In our definition we naturally view  L

Our definitions are similar to the definitions of Avdiukhin, Yaroslavtsev, Vainstein, Fischer, Das, and Mirza (2023) but applied to quartet reconstruction. In this section we extend the results of Emamjomeh-Zadeh and Kempe (2018) on the query com-plexity of learning a hierarchical clustering to the quartet (unrooted) setting. In particular, they showed that adaptive algorithms can exactly reconstruct a rooted binary hierarchy from O(n log n)

triplet queries, while any non-adaptive algorithm requires Ω( n3) triplet queries, even without noise. We show that both results naturally extend to quartets. 

Lemma E.1 (Adaptive quartets) . Assume that the responses to quartet queries are correct inde-pendently with probability p > 1/2, and adversarially incorrect otherwise. There exists an adaptive algorithm that learns the correct unrooted binary tree with probability at least 1 − δ, using at most 

O(n log n + n log(1 /δ )) quartet queries. In the absence of noise ( p = 1 ), the algorithm exactly reconstructs the tree using O(n log n) quartets. Proof. Consider the rooted tree Tr obtained by rooting T at any leaf r. For each triplet (a, b, c ), the quartet (r, a, b, c ) yields the same information: if the answer is ra |bc then (b, c ) is the closest pair in (a, b, c ), and so on. Thus, one can simulate each triplet query in the algorithm of Emamjomeh-Zadeh and Kempe (2018) by a single quartet query with r. The resulting insertion-based algorithm reconstructs Tr, hence T , exactly. In the independent noise model, the same repetition and majority-vote mechanism used in their Theorem 5.1 applies directly, preserving the O(n log n + n log(1 /δ )) 

query complexity. 

Lemma E.2 (Non-adaptive lower bound for quartets) . Any non-adaptive algorithm that recon-structs an unrooted binary tree on n leaves, even without noise, requires Ω( n3) quartet queries. Proof. Analogous to Emamjomeh-Zadeh and Kempe (2018), let C = {a, b, c, d } be one of these disjoint 4-leaf subtrees; it attaches to the rest of the tree by a single edge. Consider two global trees that are identical outside C but differ only in the internal structure within C, e.g., ab |cd vs. ac |bd .Then, any quartet query using ≤ 2 leaves from C (and the rest outside C) has the same answer under both trees.
