# Noise-Driven Differentiation via Gene Frustration and Epigenetic Fixation 

Davey Plugers ∗ and Kunihiko Kaneko †

Niels Bohr Institute, University of Copenhagen, Blegdamsvej 17, 2100 Copenhagen, Denmark 

(Dated: April 21, 2026) Gene expression in cells is stochastic, yet differentiation is robust. We propose a mechanism in which frustrated genes with weakly stable intermediate expression undergo noise-driven switching between basins of attraction, followed by irreversible fate fixation through slow epigenetic feedback. Regulatory interactions amplify effective noise and promote differentiation. We derive analytic ex-pression for the logarithmic dependence of differentiation time on noise strength and input-dependent cell-fate selection, and demonstrate homeorhesis, the dynamical robustness of the epigenetic land-scape. 

In biological systems, particularly within cells, stochas-ticity is unavoidable. As the number of molecules is not necessarily large and transcription often occurs in bursts, biochemical reactions and gene expression dynamics in-volve substantial stochasticity [1–12]. Robustness to such noise is therefore expected to have evolved to enable cells to function properly. In fact, the noise tolerance of gene expression, responses to external signals, spatial pattern-ing, and cell differentiation have been extensively inves-tigated [13–19]. The positive roles of noise in cellular function have also been discussed [20, 21], including noise- or discreteness-induced phase transitions [22], stochastic resonance [23], noise amplification [24, 25], attractor selection by noise [25–28], boundary sharpening [29], and cell-state explo-ration [30, 31]. Still, how cell differentiation can be both noise-driven and robust to noise remains elusive [32, 33]. In the clas-sical Waddington landscape, differentiation over devel-opmental time is described as a robust process termed 

homeorhesis [34], in which cell states move along branch-ing valleys. These branches correspond to differenti-ated cell types and their fractions, while the timing of branching is robust to intracellular stochasticity. Ini-tially pluripotent cells can transition into various cell fates as intracellular gene-expression patterns and chro-matin states change [35–37]. Such transitions from one to multiple stable states can be modeled through bifurcation mechanisms that repre-sent the cell-fate decision process [38–43]. However, gene expression levels within cells are noisy, and pluripotent cells must make reliable decisions near critical points for robust differentiation [31, 43–46]. This requires suitable regulatory configurations and interactions to ensure that differentiation proceeds reproducibly [47]. Recently, we discovered that differentiation from a weakly stable gene-expression state, which we term a frustrated gene-expression state, can be achieved through noise together with a slow epigenetic process that in-creases stability as the system approaches bifurcated states [48].

In the present Letter, we analyze this cell-fate deci-sion process based on frustrated gene-expression states, noise, and slow positive-feedback dynamics, and derive the conditions under which it occurs. We show that this differentiation process, although triggered by noise, is ro-bust against noise, and we derive analytic expressions for the cell-state switching time and its dependence on the input. These results are supported by numerical simula-tions, which verify the theory and demonstrate the use-fulness of the proposed mechanism for robust differentia-tion. Furthermore, we derive the Waddington epigenetic landscape over developmental time from the probability distribution of cellular states. Because this logarithmic dependence is weak, the differentiation time is not highly sensitive to these parameters, which further supports the robustness of the differentiation process. By changing the input to the gene through ci, the frac-tion of each differentiated cell type changes, and one type disappears beyond a threshold value. This behavior is also estimated analytically, and the phase diagram with respect to the threshold ci and the noise level σ is ob-tained analytically. Altogether, we derived the param-eter dependence of a robust Waddington landscape and verified the results numerically. Although we illustrated differentiation into two types using a single gene, the present study provides a basis for differentiation driven by noise and epigenetic fixation in more general and complex gene regulatory networks. The present model offers a minimal framework and can read-ily be extended to differentiation into multiple cell types. Incorporating spatial patterning and signal-induced dif-ferentiation into this framework may provide these pro-cesses with robustness to noise through θ, which will be explored in future work. In this sense, homeorhesis is expected to emerge more generally in such systems. The role of noise in generating multiple cell fates has been discussed and observed experimentally in a wide range of biological systems [4, 21, 45, 67]. One exam-ple is variability arising from bet-hedging differentiation strategies, as observed in plants [68] and microbes [69– 571]. Experimental reduction of noise has also been linked to a loss of cell fates [72], further supporting the role of noise in differentiation. Finally, the present model is consistent with experi-ments showing that stem cells exist near critical transi-tion states [73], that variability increases during transi-tions [74, 75], and that frustration can arise under multi-ple signals [76]. It is also related with theoretical studies on stochastic switching [77], network configurations con-trolling effective noise [78], and slow-fast positive feed-back with noise-induced transitions [79]. Moreover, θ,which controls cell fate and integrates gene expression over time, is consistent with recent work showing that the time integral of BMP signaling is a strong predictor of cell fate [80]. Experimental observations of intermediate chromatin states [81] also support the use of a continuous variable for epigenetic fixation.
