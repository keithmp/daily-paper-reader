# Zero-inflated modeling with smoothing on counting tensors

Abstract 

We propose a unified probabilistic framework for sparse count tensors with excess zeros, motivated by single-cell Hi-C data. The observed data are naturally represented as a three-way tensor indexed by genomic loci pairs and cells, exhibiting pronounced sparsity, zero inflation, and cell-to-cell heterogeneity. We introduce a zero-inflated Poisson tensor model that integrates low-rank CP structure, cluster-specific latent embeddings, and smoothness along ordered genomic loci, thereby jointly capturing multiway dependence, heterogeneity, and structured variation. We develop a Bayes-optimal procedure for distinguishing structural from techni-cal zeros, enabling principled inference and uncertainty quantification. We establish identifiability of the model parameters and derive consistency rates for the proposed estimators in a high-dimensional regime. Simulation studies and analyses of single-cell Hi-C data demonstrate improved performance in zero detection, latent structure recov-ery, and downstream tasks such as clustering and 3D chromatin organization inference. The proposed framework provides a flexible approach for multiway count data with excess zeros and structured dependencies, and suggests several directions for future work, including mixture-based modeling of cell populations and scalable computation for large-scale applications. 

Keywords: Counting Tensors, Zero-inflation, False Zero Detection, Imputation 1

# 1 Introduction 

High-dimensional count data with excess zeros arise in many modern applications, including single-cell genomics (Nagano et al., 2017; Pierson and Yau, 2015), ecology (Martin et al., 2005; Zuur et al., 2009), and recommender systems with implicit feedback (Hu et al., 2008). Understanding the mechanisms generating excess zeros is essential for recovering the under-lying signal, since observed zeros may arise from multiple sources, including true structural absence, technical limitations, detection failure, and measurement noise (Hicks et al., 2018). A substantial literature has addressed zero inflation in lower-dimensional settings through hurdle and zero-inflated models that explicitly separate structural and sampling zeros (Mul-lahy, 1986; Lambert, 1992; Cameron and Trivedi, 2013). These approaches are well estab-lished for univariate and low-dimensional count data. In parallel, tensor methodology has emerged as a flexible framework for modeling multiway data via low-rank representations (Kolda and Bader, 2009; De Lathauwer et al., 2000). However, existing tensor decomposition methods typically do not accommodate excess zeros, while classical zero-inflated models do not naturally extend to tensor-valued observations. Bridging these two literatures is therefore essential for analyzing modern multiway count data. In this paper, we study inference for sparse count tensors under zero inflation as a pri-mary modeling challenge. We propose a zero-inflated Poisson tensor model in which both the intensity and masking probability tensors admit low-rank structure. Beyond zero infla-tion, the proposed framework accommodates additional structures commonly encountered in multiway data. In particular, different modes may exhibit distinct forms of organization: one mode may index heterogeneous units with latent group structure, while another may correspond to an ordered domain along which the underlying signal varies smoothly. These components are incorporated through structured latent representations that jointly capture heterogeneity across units and smooth variation along ordered modes, yielding a flexible 2model for complex tensor-valued count data. A key challenge is that existing approaches address these components in isolation. Zero-inflated regression models accommodate excess zeros but assume independent observations and do not extend to tensor-valued data (Mullahy, 1986; Lambert, 1992). Tensor decom-position methods capture low-rank multiway dependence but typically rely on standard likelihood models that do not account for zero inflation (Kolda and Bader, 2009). At the same time, probabilistic tensor models generally impose a shared distributional form across slices or modes, limiting their ability to capture heterogeneity across subpopulations (Hoff, 2011). Smoothing methods for ordered or spatial domains are well developed in classical regression settings (Wood, 2017), but are largely disconnected from tensor factorization and zero-inflated frameworks. Consequently, a unified approach that simultaneously accommo-dates zero inflation, low-rank multiway dependence, heterogeneity, and smooth structure remains lacking. 

Motivating application A key application motivating this work is single-cell chromatin contact data obtained via Hi-C technology (Lieberman-Aiden et al., 2009; Rao et al., 2014). Hi-C is a genomic assay that measures the frequency of spatial proximity between pairs of genomic loci and is widely used to reconstruct three-dimensional (3D) chromatin organization (Duan et al. (2010); Hu et al. (2013) and, more recently, Tuzhilina et al. (2022, 2024)), with broad applications to gene regulation, chromosome architecture, and disease studies. The output of Hi-C is a symmetric, integer-valued contact matrix whose entries record the observed interaction frequencies between pairs of genomic loci, with higher values typically interpreted as indicating closer spatial proximity. Early work focused on bulk Hi-C data, which aggregate signals across many cells into a single contact matrix whose entries represent interaction frequencies at the population level. More recently, single-cell Hi-C technologies enable measurements at the level of in-3dividual cells, yielding a collection of contact matrices that capture cell-to-cell variability in chromatin organization (Nagano et al., 2017; Stevens et al., 2017; Ramani et al., 2017). These data naturally form a three-way tensor (genomic loci Г— genomic loci Г— cell). While enabling characterization of cell-to-cell heterogeneity in chromatin organization, single-cell measurements also introduce substantial statistical challenges. 1. Extreme sparsity: Single-cell contact matrices are highly sparse due to both biolog-ical absence of contacts and technical limitations such as low sequencing depth and incomplete capture efficiency. Disentangling structural zeros (true absence of contact) from technical zeros (dropout events) is essential for downstream tasks such as 3D reconstruction. Failure to distinguish these sources can bias inference, while treating all zeros as missing discards informative structural signal. 2. Cell-to-cell heterogeneity: Chromatin organization exhibits variability across cells. Statistical methods must therefore accommodate population heterogeneity while bor-rowing strength across cells through shared structure. 3. Smoothness along the genome: Genomic loci are ordered along chromosomes, and neighboring loci tend to exhibit similar interaction patterns due to the continuous nature of chromatin. Leveraging this smoothness can improve signal recovery. Existing methods address these challenges in isolation. HiCImpute (Xie et al., 2022) distinguishes structural zeros from technical dropouts via a Bayesian hierarchical model. scHiCSRS (Xu et al., 2022) combines self-representation smoothing with mixture modeling for zero identification. Higashi (Zhang et al., 2022) employs hypergraph neural networks to learn cell embeddings and impute contact maps by leveraging correlations across cells. However, these approaches lack a unified probabilistic framework that exploits the inherent tensor structure of the data and jointly models zero inflation, cell-to-cell heterogeneity, and smoothness along the genomic loci. 4Proposed framework and main contributions. We propose Zero-Inflated Tensor model with Smoothing ( ZITS ), a likelihood-based framework for sparse count tensors integrating three components: (i) zero-inflated Poisson model with entry-specific masking probabilities to explicitly separate technical and structural zeros; (ii) low-rank CP tensor decomposi-tion with heterogeneous structure across one mode via latent grouping; and (iii) functional smoothing along ordered modes via basis expansion. 1. Theoretical contributions: We establish identifiability conditions for the model pa-rameters and derive consistency rates for the maximum likelihood estimator. Notably, despite O(K) parameter growth with sample size, consistent estimation is achievable through the block structure constraint, providing theoretical justification for the model design. 2. Methodological contributions: We develop a likelihood-based estimation algorithm and propose a Bayes-optimal classifier for distinguishing structural from technical zeros, enabling principled zero-inflation detection with posterior uncertainty quantification. The unified probabilistic framework allows joint inference over all model components. 3. Empirical validation: We demonstrate effectiveness through simulation studies and real single-cell Hi-C data, showing improved signal recovery, enhanced zero detection, and more accurate latent representations compared to existing approaches. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. We begin in Section 2 by introduc-ing the proposed model, followed by Section 3, which develops the estimation and inference procedures. The theoretical properties are studied in Section 4, where we establish identi-fiability and consistency and introduce the zero classification and imputation method. We then assess the performance of the approach through simulation studies in Section 5 and illustrate its practical utility on real single-cell Hi-C data in Section 6. In this paper, we developed a unified probabilistic framework for sparse count tensors with excess zeros. The proposed approach integrates a zero-inflated Poisson observation model, low-rank CP tensor decomposition, cluster-specific latent embeddings to capture hetero-geneity, and functional smoothing along ordered modes. This integration provides a flexible framework for addressing key challenges in modern multiway count data. We develop a Bayes-optimal classifier for distinguishing structural from technical zeros, enabling principled inference on zero inflation and uncertainty quantification. We estab-lish identifiability of the model parameters and derive consistency rates for the proposed estimators. Simulation studies and analyses of single-cell Hi-C data demonstrate improved performance in signal recovery, zero detection, and latent structure inference compared to existing methods. Although motivated by chromatin contact data, the proposed framework extends natu-rally to other multiway count data with zero inflation, heterogeneity, and smooth structure, such as ecological data indexed by species, location, time, and environmental conditions. Several directions remain for future work. First, while heterogeneity is currently captured via latent embeddings and post hoc clustering, a more principled approach would model cluster membership directly through mixture models for tensor-valued data, enabling explicit probabilistic characterization of subpopulations. Second, scaling the method to ultra-high-dimensional settings such as high-resolution Hi-C data may require distributed optimization and parallel computation. Finally, extending the framework to higher-order tensors and developing data-driven procedures for selecting model complexity, such as the number of latent factors, are important directions for future investigation. 34 References
