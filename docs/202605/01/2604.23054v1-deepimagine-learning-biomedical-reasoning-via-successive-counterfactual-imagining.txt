# Abstract 

Predicting the outcomes of prospective clinical trials remains a major challenge for large language models (LLMs). Prior work has shown that both traditional correlational predictors, such as random forests and logistic regression, and strong commercial LLMs achieve limited performance on this task. In this paper, we pro-pose DeepImagine , a framework for teaching LLMs biomedical reasoning through successive counterfactual imagining. The central idea is to approximate hidden causal mechanisms of clinical trials by training models to infer how observed trial results would change under controlled perturbations of experimental conditions, such as dosage, outcome measures, study arms, geography, and other trial attributes. To support this objective, we construct both natural and approximate counterfac-tual pairs from real clinical trials with reported outcomes. For settings where strict counterfactual supervision is available, such as paired outcome measures or dose-ranging study arms within the same trial, we train models with supervised fine-tuning. For broader settings where only approximate counterfactual pairs can be retrieved, we optimize models with reinforcement learning using verifiable rewards based on downstream benchmark correctness. We further augment training with synthetic reasoning traces that provide causally plausible explanations for local counterfactual transitions. Using this pipeline, we train language models under 10B parameters, including Qwen3.5-9B, and evaluate them on clinical trial outcome prediction. We aim to show that DeepImagine consistently improves over untuned language models and traditional correlational baselines. Finally, we aim to show that the learned reasoning trajectories provide interpretable signals about how models represent trial-level mechanisms, suggesting a practical path toward more mechanistic and scientifically useful biomedical language models. 

# 1 Introduction 

Clinical trial outcomes are influenced by a wide range of factors. Some of these are relatively observable, including enrollment size, trial phase, location, and sponsor characteristics. Larger enrollment can increase statistical power, making it easier to detect statistically significant effects. Phase III trials often face a higher threshold for success, as they represent one of the final stages before a drug can receive FDA approval. Trial location may also matter, since studies conducted in developed countries may benefit from stronger procedural adherence and tighter control over participants than those conducted in developing countries. Sponsor characteristics can further shape

outcomes, as larger companies often have greater resources, more experienced research teams, and more mature development pipelines than small or mid-sized firms. Despite the importance of these factors, it remains difficult to formulate an exact equation that specifies how they interact and jointly determine the outcome of a clinical trial. In addition, many non-clinical external factors may also affect trial results, although such factors are outside the scope of this work. Still, we hypothesize that clinical trial outcomes are ultimately governed by underlying laws and mechanisms, possibly with some degree of randomness, which means that there should exist a hidden set of causal laws and mechanisms that determines how these factors jointly produce observed results. If such laws and mechanisms could be identified, they could enable highly accurate prediction of trial outcomes. In this paper, we refer to this latent set of governing laws and mechanisms as the hidden causal model of clinical trial outcomes. The central insight of this paper is that LLMs can improve prediction of a prospective clinical trial when the target trial can be linked to many highly similar historical trials with reported outcomes, and when the model is allowed to condition on those prior results. Although the success of a previous trial does not guarantee the success of a new one, we find that trials associated with a larger fraction of successful predecessors tend to have a higher probability of success. Conventional correlational methods would typically encode this signal as a numerical feature, such as the proportion of related prior trials that succeeded. In contrast, we hypothesize that an LLM can use these prior trials more mechanistically, by reasoning through a sequence of counterfactual imaginations. Specifically, beginning with a historical trial whose outcome is known, we change one experimental condition at a time to match the target trial, keeping all remaining conditions fixed, and ask the model to infer how the known outcome would change under that perturbation. By repeating this process over all differing factors, the model effectively traverses a chain of counterfactual states from the historical trial to the target trial, and the final imagined outcome becomes the prediction. See Figure 1 for an example of successive counterfactual imagination. In this paper, we propose a training method that enables LLMs to gradually internalize these governing mechanisms. Our approach trains models on coupled experiments, where all experimental conditions are kept fixed except for a single factor. For instance, we provide a sequence of experiments with identical patient demographics, intervention drugs, locations, study designs, and outcome measures, while varying only the dosage from 10 mg daily to 100 mg daily. Importantly, each experiment in this sequence is drawn from a real clinical study with reported results. As a result, although the experiments function as counterfactual variants of one another for training purposes, they are all grounded in real-world outcomes. We train the model via supervised fine-tuning using these data. For many variables, corresponding counterfactuals are unavailable. In this work, we use the term counterfactual to refer to a pair of clinical trial experiments in which only one condition differs while all other conditions are held fixed. In practice, this type of strict counterfactual is rarely available, because it would require a real clinical trial to have been conducted under an alternative version of the same experimental setup, with every aspect unchanged except for the single variable of interest. Consequently, although such counterfactual settings can be easily imagined, their true outcomes are usually unobservable. To address this limitation, we develop an information retrieval pipeline that links clinical trials with highly similar experimental settings, enabling the construction of approximate counterfactual pairs grounded in real reported outcomes. Empirically, we find that these pairings often differ in only a small number of variables. For example, a company may conduct a successful Phase II trial and subsequently launch a Phase III trial for the same drug. Although both trials involve the same intervention, they may differ in success criteria, such as outcome measures, and the Phase III trial may enroll a larger and more heterogeneous patient population. Using such pairings, we train the model to discover its own reasoning traces, rewarding it solely for its ability to predict the outcome of one trial from the other to a meaningful degree. Taken together, these ideas suggest a different role for language models in clinical trial prediction: rather than acting purely as correlational predictors, they can be trained to approximate the latent causal mechanisms that govern how trial outcomes change under controlled perturbations of experi-mental conditions. Building on this view, we introduce DeepImagine, a framework that combines natural and approximate counterfactual trial pairings, synthetic causal reasoning traces, and reinforce-ment learning over verifiable outcomes. Through this combination, we aim to teach LLMs to perform successive counterfactual imagination as a practical approximation to biomedical causal reasoning. Empirically, we show that this approach improves prospective clinical trial outcome prediction over 2Figure 1: An example of Successive Counterfactual Imagination that allows a model to predict the target trial’s results based on the known historical trial’s results through a series of predictions. both traditional correlational baselines and untuned language models, while also producing reasoning traces that can be analyzed to recover interpretable structured causal models. 

# 2 Related Work 

Causal Inference: Our work is grounded in the structural causal model (SCM) view of causality, where causal questions are framed in terms of interventions and counterfactuals rather than correla-tions [ 19 ]. In particular, Pearl’s abduction–action–prediction formulation motivates our notion of successive counterfactual imagining. Unlike classical causal inference, however, we do not assume access to a fully specified SCM for clinical trials; instead, we train a language model to approximate counterfactual transitions from real and approximate trial pairs. 

Causal LLM and Techniques to Train Them: Recent work has examined whether LLMs can perform causal reasoning reliably. Kıcıman et al. [11] show that strong LLMs can often produce plausible causal arguments but remain brittle. Jin et al. [10] introduced CLadder and showed that formally grounded causal reasoning remains difficult for current models. Related benchmarks extend this direction: ExpliCa evaluates explicit causal and temporal reasoning [ 15 ], CounterBench studies formal counterfactual inference [ 3], and executable counterfactual work argues that many settings overestimate counterfactual ability by bypassing abduction [ 24 ]. At the training level, process supervision has been shown to improve multi-step reasoning relative to pure outcome supervision [14 ]. Our work builds on these ideas but focuses on biomedical forecasting, where LLMs reason over linked clinical trials rather than abstract causal tasks. 

Clinical Trial Benchmarks: Clinical trial outcome prediction has been studied through both specialized models and benchmark frameworks. HINT uses multimodal trial representations for outcome prediction [ 4], while SPOT models sequential relations among similar historical trials [ 31 ]. PyTrial provides a broader framework for machine learning on clinical-trial tasks, including outcome prediction and similarity search [ 30 ]. On the data side, CTO offers a large-scale clinical trial outcome resource [ 6], while CT Open introduces an open, time-stamped, decontaminated benchmark for prospective outcome prediction [ 28 ]. Our work is most closely aligned with CT Open because we focus on prospective, uncontaminated evaluation. Our retrieval component is also related to Trial2Vec [ 29 ], though we use similar trials not only for retrieval but also as anchors for successive counterfactual imagination. 3Reinforcement Learning: Our reinforcement-learning stage builds on policy optimization and RL-for-LLM training. PPO introduced the clipped surrogate objective that remains a standard method for stable policy optimization [ 20 ]. In language modeling, reinforcement learning from feedback was popularized by work on summarization with human feedback [ 22 ] and InstructGPT [ 18 ]. More recently, DeepSeekMath proposed Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), which removes the separate critic and uses group-relative rewards for more memory-efficient reasoning-oriented training [ 21 ]. We adopt GRPO in a different regime, where the model receives reward only for terminal correctness in clinical trial prediction and must discover useful latent counterfactual trajectories. 

Synthetic Data Generation: Synthetic data generation has been widely used for retrieval and question answering when labeled supervision is limited. IR2 studies synthetic-query generation for retrieval under low-resource settings [ 25 ], while single-pass document scanning make an innovative use of linked data to make synthetic data to teach model the ability of long-range reasoning [ 1 ]. Other work improves diversity and task alignment through retrieved or transformed supervision, including DataTune [ 5] and Genie [ 32 ]. Synthetic data has also been used to build compositional supervision for multi-hop QA [ 2 ]. Our setting differs in that we generate supervision for counterfactual transitions between highly similar clinical trials rather than standalone QA pairs, while aligning to the critical insight in [1] that shows pairing existing data in new ways could teach model new skills. 

Biomedical Reasoning: Biomedical reasoning has been evaluated through both broad NLP bench-marks and specialized evidence-centric tasks. BLURB provides a general biomedical benchmark suite [8 ], while PubMedQA and BioASQ-QA evaluate literature-grounded biomedical question answering [9, 12 ]. More recent benchmarks target higher-level scientific reasoning: EvidenceBench evaluates evidence retrieval and synthesis for biomedical hypotheses [ 27 ], and RoBBR measures risk-of-bias estimation in biomedical studies [ 26 ]. These tasks align with the broader tradition of evidence-based biomedical reasoning and critical appraisal [ 7]. In contrast, we study whether LLMs can use linked historical trials to perform successive counterfactual imagination for prospective clinical trial outcome prediction.

3.1 Counterfactual Imagining in Causal Inference 

In causal inference, counterfactual reasoning asks what would have happened to an outcome had some aspect of the world been different, while all other causally relevant mechanisms remained governed by the same underlying system. In the structural causal model (SCM) framework of Pearl, a causal model is typically written as 

M = ( U, V, F, P (U )) ,

where U denotes exogenous variables, V denotes endogenous variables, F is a collection of struc-tural assignments, and P (U ) specifies the distribution over background factors. An intervention 

do (X = x) replaces the structural equation for X with the assignment X = x, thereby defining a modified submodel in which the downstream consequences of that intervention can be evaluated. Counterfactual quantities are then written as expressions such as Yx, denoting the value that Y would attain under the intervention do (X = x), and more generally as conditional probabilities of the form 

P (Yx = y | e),

where e denotes factual evidence about the realized world. A standard account of counterfactual evaluation in Pearl’s framework proceeds in three steps: ab-duction, action, and prediction. In the abduction step, the model updates its beliefs about latent background variables using the observed evidence e. In the action step, it modifies the model by imposing the hypothetical intervention do (X = x). In the prediction step, it computes the resulting outcome in the modified model. Thus, counterfactual inference is not merely prediction under a new input; rather, it is prediction for the same underlying unit after minimally altering the relevant causal mechanism specified by the antecedent. This distinction is important because counterfactuals inherit information from the factual world through abduction before the hypothetical change is applied. Motivated by this tradition, we use the term counterfactual imagining to denote a model-side ap-proximation to counterfactual inference: starting from an observed clinical trial result, the model 4imagines how that result would change if one trial condition were perturbed while all other relevant conditions were held fixed. Formally, if x(0) denotes a source trial configuration with observed result 

R(0) , and x(T ) denotes a target configuration, then a successive counterfactual imagination procedure constructs a path 

x(0) , x (1) , . . . , x (T ),

where each consecutive pair differs in exactly one experimental condition. The model then generates a corresponding sequence 

R(1) , R (2) , . . . , R (T ),

with each R(t) interpreted as the imagined result obtained after applying a local perturbation from 

x(t−1) to x(t). In this sense, our formulation can be viewed as a sequential approximation to a complex counterfactual query: instead of directly estimating the target result from scratch, the model traverses a chain of local “what-if” transformations from the observed source trial to the unobserved target trial. Our use of counterfactual imagining is therefore inspired by, but not identical to, classical SCM-based counterfactual inference. In the ideal SCM formulation, a counterfactual such as 

P (Rx′ | e)

is defined with respect to a fully specified causal model and evaluated through abduction, action, and prediction. In our setting, the true causal law is unknown and only partial supervision is available. We therefore train a language model Pθ to approximate this process through successive local transformations, using observed source results as factual evidence and trial-pair differences as approximate interventions. The goal is not to claim access to the full SCM of clinical trials, but rather to induce a practically useful approximation to counterfactual reasoning that preserves the central Pearlian intuition: infer what would happen under a hypothetical change by anchoring the computation in what is already known about the factual case. 

3.2 Clinical Trial Terminology 

We first give a quick introduction to terminology used in clinical trial outcome prediction, following the convention of CT Open. 

Definition (Intervention/Treatment) . For clinical trials, an intervention, or treatment, refers to the therapeutic regimen being evaluated. This may consist of a single drug, a combination of drugs, a medical procedure, or a combination of drugs and procedures. Example: A trial evaluating pembrolizumab alone, or pembrolizumab combined with chemotherapy, would treat these regimens as interventions. 

Definition (Study, Treatment, and Comparator Arms) . A study arm is a group of participants assigned to a particular condition within a clinical trial. A treatment arm receives the intervention being tested, whereas a comparator arm serves as the control condition, such as a placebo, standard of care, or another treatment. Example: In a two-arm trial, one group may receive a new cancer drug, while the comparator arm receives standard chemotherapy. 

Definition (Outcome Measure and Endpoint) . An outcome measure, also called an endpoint, is a pre-specified quantity used to evaluate the effect of an intervention in a clinical trial. It defines what will be measured and is part of the trial design, rather than the observed trial result. Example: The 5-year overall survival rate is an outcome measure. 

Definition (Result and Outcome) . A result, or outcome, is the observed value of an outcome measure after the trial has been conducted. It describes what actually happened in the trial. Example: “85% of patients survived for at least 5 years after treatment” is a result, whereas “5-year overall survival rate” is the corresponding outcome measure. 4.1 Discovering Natural Counterfactuals 

In practice, a clinical trial often includes multiple outcome measures and multiple study arms. On average, a trial contains roughly two primary outcome measures and eight to ten secondary outcome measures, reflecting the fact that clinical studies are typically designed to evaluate several aspects of an intervention rather than a single effect. Controlled trials contain at least two arms: a comparator arm, often placebo or standard of care, and an intervention arm. In many cases, however, a trial includes more than two arms. Beyond the baseline comparator arm, there may be multiple treatment arms, each corresponding to a different intervention regimen. For example, one arm may administer 10 mg of a drug per day, another 20 mg per day, and a third 30 mg per day. This type of design is commonly referred to as a dose-ranging or dosing study, in which investigators seek to determine whether treatment effects become more pronounced as dosage increases. 4.2 Approximating Other Counterfactuals 

For all remaining variables, we absorb them into the clinical trial symbol C. These variables include, among others, the drug’s biological mechanism of action, geography (i.e., the countries in which participating hospitals administer the intervention), and patient eligibility criteria, whose inclusion and exclusion rules jointly define the health profile of the enrolled population. In general, natural counterfactuals for these variables are difficult to obtain. Nevertheless, a few important exceptions do arise in practice. First, the baseline comparator is sometimes not a placebo but an established drug. In such cases, the comparator and the investigational drug often share certain similarities while still differing in clinically meaningful ways. These pairings may therefore encourage the model to internally represent how trial results change when the intervention drug changes while other aspects of the trial remain similar. Second, a clinical trial conducted in a single country may later motivate a follow-up trial with an otherwise similar design but expanded to multiple countries or conducted globally. Such pairings provide a natural counterfactual setting for studying how changes in geography affect trial outcomes. Outside these special cases, natural counterfactuals rarely exist for most trial variables. We therefore construct approximate counterfactual pairs using a combination of LLM-based information retrieval and FDA drug monographs. For each retrieved pair of trials, Ci1 and Ci2 , the corresponding trial attributes are highly similar overall, but several variables may differ simultaneously. In this setting, it is no longer feasible to construct supervision signals for a full sequence of one-variable perturbations, since the intermediate counterfactual outcomes are not observed in reality. # 5 Experiment Setup 

5.1 Evaluation Dataset Construction 

We evaluate our approach by its downstream utility for clinical trial outcome prediction. From the training split of [ 28 ], we draw 250 random questions from each of the Superiority and Comparative Effect classes of the clinical trial outcome prediction task. To further assess the quality of the two counterfactual perturbation independently, we construct two dedicated evaluation datasets from this sample: 

Outcome-measure perturbation set. For each (trial, outcome measure) pair in the evaluation sample, we identify the most similar alternative outcome measure within the same trial, ranked by cosine similarity of the embeddings from [ 13 ]. A evaluated model is provided with the reported result of the nearest outcome measure and asked to predict the target outcome measure’s result under the same study arm. 

Study-arm perturbation set. Each evaluation question is associated with two study arms reported under a common outcome measure. We fix one of these arms and pair it with a different study arm drawn from the same trial that was measured under the same outcome. A evaluated model is provided with the paired arm’s reported result and asked to predict the fixed arm’s result. 

5.2 Model Fine-Tuning 

Following the methodology in §4.1, we use gpt-5.4-2026-03-05 [ 17 ], with medium reasoning effort and low verbosity, to generate synthetic counterfactual reasoning trajectories for study-arm and outcome-measure perturbations, giving about 26k trajectories for each perturbation type. We fine-tuned Qwen-3.5-9B [ 23 ] with LoRA on each of the arm-perturbation and outcome-measure perturbation sets. LoRA adapters were applied to all linear layers with rank r = 32 , scaling factor 

α = 64 , and dropout 0.05 . We trained for one epoch with a per-device batch size of 1 and gradient accumulation of 2. Optimization used AdamW with a peak learning rate of 5 × 10 −5, cosine decay to a minimum 0.1 of the peak learning rate, 5% warm-up steps, zero weight decay, and gradient clipping at 1.0. The maximum sequence length was 8,192 tokens. Training was performed on a single node with 7x80GB H100 GPUs using FlashAttention-4. See Table 1 for detailed training hyperparameter settings. # 7 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced DeepImagine, a framework for teaching language models biomedical reasoning through successive counterfactual imagining. Our central premise is that, although the true causal laws governing clinical trial outcomes remain hidden, highly similar historical trials with reported results can provide useful anchors for approximating how outcomes change under controlled perturbations of trial conditions. By combining natural counterfactual supervision, approximate counterfactual retrieval, synthetic reasoning traces, and reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, we train language models to predict prospective clinical trial outcomes through sequences of local counterfactual transitions rather than one-shot correlational prediction. Empirically, this approach improves performance over conventional correlational baselines and untuned language models, while also yielding interpretable reasoning traces that can be analyzed to recover structured causal hypotheses. More broadly, our results suggest that counterfactual imagination may provide a practical path toward more mechanistic and scientifically useful reasoning in biomedical language models. 12 References
