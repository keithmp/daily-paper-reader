# Modeling the impact of host diversity on the evolution of vector feeding preferences and implications for disease control 

Shravani Shetgaonkar, Anupama Sharma 1

> Department of Mathematics, Birla Institute of Technology and Science, Pilani, K K Birla Goa Campus, Zuarinagar, Sancoale, Goa 403726, India,

Abstract 

Vector-borne diseases often infect multiple host species, increasing the likelihood of disease per-sistence due to the presence of multiple reservoirs. Vector biting patterns and feeding preferences can shift in response to selective pressures introduced by disease control interventions, altering the dynamics of transmission. In this paper, we develop a mathematical model that couples host diversity and adaptive vector behavior with vector-borne disease transmission dynamics, focusing on a system with two hosts and a vector population exhibiting preference for one host. We derive the basic reproduction number, R0, a threshold that determines the existence of two equilibria in our model, and obtain conditions that can lead to the long-term persistence of the disease. Our analysis suggests that shortening the infectious period of the vector’s preferred host is an effective control strategy. We also identified a threshold that determines whether shifting vector preference toward a non-preferred host amplifies or reduces the disease burden on the primary preferred host. Our results show that protective measures for the preferred host can trigger adaptive shifts in vector preferences, reducing disease prevalence in that host. However, this shift may lead to an increase in overall host prevalence. 

Keywords: Compartmental model, vector borne disease, biting behavior, vector preference, endemic equilibrium, evolutionary dynamics. 

1. Introduction 

Vector feeding preference refers to the tendency of vector species to feed more frequently on a particular host compared to others. Various genetic and physiological host traits, such as odor, body heat, and body mass, shape the vector feeding preferences [1]. For instance, Aedes aegypti, 

which are vectors for diseases like chikungunya, dengue fever, yellow fever, and Zika, exhibit a strong preference for feeding on humans over other animals [2]. Similar patterens have been doc-umented for vectors of Lyme disease [3], West Nile virus (WNV) [4, 5], and Chagas disease [6]. These feeding preferences not only influence the distribution and intensity of disease transmission, but can also impact the effectiveness of control measures. Furthermore, pathogens can exhibit antigenic variation as an adaptive response to the diverse immune defenses of multiple host species [7]. For instance, African trypanosomiasis, transmitted by the tsetse fly and caused by the pro-tozoan parasite Trypanosoma brucei, is characterized by the parasite’s ability to switch between different antigenic variants. This switch allows the parasite to evade the host’s immune system,

contributing to the chronic nature of the infection [8]. Such antigenic variations can also lead to the development of drug resistance in pathogens [9, 10]. When multiple host species participate in the pathogen infection cycle, one species may act as the primary reservoir, sustaining the pathogen and serving as a continuous source of infection to other hosts. However, most mathematical models for vector-borne disease (VBD) have primarily focused on single-host single-vector frameworks [11–16]. A comprehensive understanding of vector-host interactions is essential for designing targeted prevention and control measures [17, 18], as the population composition, i.e., abundance of different hosts, affects the vectorial capacity and alter the course of disease spread. For example, increasing the proportion of incompetent hosts – the host unable to transmit the disease even when bitten by the vector – has been shown to reduce the prevalence of Chagas disease [19]. Ignoring vector feeding preferences in models with diverse host populations can lead to incomplete understanding of transmission dynamics and result in sub-optimal control measures. Establishing a unifying principle of how host diversity influences VBD transmission is chal-lenging, as its effect on disease dynamics is largely shaped by the ecological specifics of each system [20–25]. For example, whether host diversity amplifies, dilutes, or has no effect on disease prevalence depends on the interplay between vector preference and host transmission abilities, with amplification occurring when both host species are present leading to maximal disease risk [26]. Moreover, vectors may adapt their feeding preferences in response to host specific interventions, creating significant bottlenecks for the control and elimination of VBD. When vectors initially bite only competent hosts, time-dependent feeding preferences can increase disease prevalence and shift the timing of epidemic peaks [27]. Hence, understanding the evolutionary dynamics of vector preference and its effect on disease transmission is crucial for disease control. In this study, we examine the long-term implications of vector preference on VBD transmission and study the relationship between host diversity and the evolution of vector feeding preferences using adaptive dynamics [28]. Specifically, we analyze how changes in host community composition affect the evolutionary trait of vectors to feed on a non-preferred host. We sought to quantify the relationship between adaptive responses driven by the trade-off between a preference for a specific host and the need to evade the defences of alternative hosts. We found that vector preferences can shift in response to protective measures employed by the preferred host, which may lead to an overall increase in disease prevalence amongst the host species. Our approach offers a framework to evaluate the effectiveness of implementing interventions such as ITN and vector repellents for abating prevalence among h1 in the presence of h2. The paper is structured as follows: In Section 2 we formulate a compartmental model for VBD with two hosts, one vector, and vector preference. Section 3 covers the computation of R0, and the analysis of equilibria and their stability. Section 4 provides a sensitivity analysis of R0. Section 5 explores the evolutionary dynamics of vector feeding preferences using adaptive dynamics, and Section 7 concludes with a discussion of the results.

5. Evolutionary dynamics of vector biting pattern and host-seeking behavior 

In a single-host system, protecting the host can effectively reduce disease prevalence. However, this principle does not extend to multi-host systems such as ours, where community composition becomes a critical determinant of disease risk. In a scenario where one host becomes “functionally unavailable" to the pathogen – for example, one host population using protection against vector – an evolutionary question arises: whether selection will favor convergence toward a generalist phenotype capable of exploiting multiple host types, or specialization and host preference will be maintained? Moreover, how would such evolutionary shifts will affect the long-term dynamics of disease transmission? To answer these questions, we investigate the adaptive dynamics of vector populations for the evolution of their feeding preferences. Furthermore, to verify whether the disease risk among h1

reduces under such conditions, we investigate the impact of the evolution of a generalist strategy on the dynamics of VBD. 7. Discussion 

The diversity - stability debate in ecology is a classic and ongoing discussion about how biodi-versity affects the stability of ecosystems. Similarly, epidemiologists continue to examine whether the increased host diversity can amplify or dilute the prevalence of a VBD. Understanding this complex dynamics is crucial from policy making viewpoint as interventions designed with the focus only on a single host species are known to shift vector preference and impact the broader disease ecology [34, 35]. Our work is a step towards providing a unified framework that integrates host-vector interactions with vector preference adaptation, which are measurable characteristics of host and vector populations. Our model analysis teases out conditions where transmission intensifies 17 when vectors with feeding preferences spread the disease between two hosts. We have also illus-trated that R0 only reaches its minimum value R0c when the vector preference equals to measure of host competence of h2 relative to h1. Additionally, we found that the effect of host population size on R0 is not straightforward, which complements the findings by [26]. We demonstrated that an increase in host population size reduces the spread of VBD when the recovery rate for that host exceeds a certain threshold, which explains the previous results showing that an abundance of the low competent host population can reduce VBD transmission [35]. Our results suggest that a strong vector preference for a particular host population ( αv ≫ 1 for h1 and αv ≪ 1 for h2)can hinder disease eradication. However, effective treatment that shortens the infectious period of the preferred host can increase the recovery rate above the threshold, reducing VBD prevalence. Importantly, we have illustrated that by monitoring vector feeding patterns and observing reservoir host populations near human communities, this threshold can be identified, which will enabling timely interventions to facilitate VBD eradication. A key result of our analysis is the derivation of a critical threshold, R0c, that characterizes the impact of vector preference on the equilibrium prevalence of h1. This threshold marks a transition in the qualitative behavior of the system and provides a mechanistic basis for anticipating shifts in disease dynamics. R0c may serve as a valuable indicator for informing surveillance efforts and optimizing control strategies, particularly in systems where vector behavior is variable or subject to intervention. We found that when h1 is a highly competent host (owing to high values of βhv and 

βvh , along with a low recovery rate μ1), the prevalence in h1 increases steadily as vector preference 

αv shifts toward it. Interestingly, in this setting, the presence of a second host, h2, with lower competence, can help reduce the infection level in h1, particularly if some proportion of vector bites are redirected toward h2. The effect is more noticeable when h2 contributes little to vector infection, acting more as a biological buffer than a source. Diverting vectors from humans to animals as a strategy to mitigate vector-borne disease (VBD) transmission has significant empirical support [36]. The concept of zooprophylaxis - leveraging alternative host species to reduce human-vector interactions - has emerged as a promising approach in VBD management. Moreover, integrating zooprophylaxis with targeted insecticide application to protect these additional host species offers a dual benefit: reducing vector populations while simultaneously disrupting disease transmission cycles [37, 38]. Recent studies have found functions of vector preference and transmission ratio to predict am-plification and dilution effects in the presence of multiple hosts [24, 26]. Moreover, these conditions are established on the basis of whether species richness increases or decreases R0 and fail to address its effects on prevalence among a particular species. Our findings indicate that depending on the value R0c, increasing vector preference towards h2 can trigger a resurgence of VBD prevalence in 

h1, y∗ 

> 1

. Such behavior is observed from Figure 3(a)-(b). That is when R0c is near 1 and αv is decreasing below αvc then y∗ 

> 1

increases and attains a maximum at some point in (0 , α vc ). This suggests that protecting humans ( h1) can concentrate bites on surrounding unprotected hosts and result in increased infections. Our result agrees with the fact that when R0c is low the abundance of h2 can cause amplification of infected cases [39]. Moreover, our results also indicate that the introduction of secondary hosts like livestock in the presence of vectors that show moderate pref-erence can increase the transmission of VBD. Such a outcome has been witnessed for Malaria, [40]. Hence, conducting a complete evaluation of factors, such as host recovery rate, transmission efficiency, and vector feeding behavior is advised before implementing the intervention strategy to divert vector preference for its effectiveness in controlling VBD. Our model simulations showed a decline in prevalence among h1, while prevalence in h2 and the vector increased, as shown in Figure 7(f). Hence our approach also offers a framework to evaluate the effectiveness of implementing 18 interventions such as ITN and vector repellents for abating prevalence among h1 in the presence of h2.Studies incorporating vector preference into their transmission terms [19, 21, 22, 27] have overlooked how changes in the underlying factors that influence vector preference can lead to adverse outcomes. Our work fills this knowledge gap by identifying the conditions under which vector preference may shift from h1 to h2, while explicitly linking vector demographics to their feeding behaviour. Our results reveal that vector populations tend to evolve toward one of two extremes: a specialist strategy where vectors feed exclusively on h1, or a generalist strategy in which vectors consistently bite h2 whenever possible. The direction of evolution depends on the initial trait distribution which separates the basins of attraction for these two strategies on the Pairwise Invasibility Plot (PIP). By examining how the singular strategy varies with trade-off strength, we found that the probability of a host being targeted by vectors depends not only on its abundance but also on the “cost’ of specialization and the host’s defensive capabilities. In particular, we find that a high feeding preference for h1 can be redirected toward h2 when the encounter rate with h2

equals or exceeds that of h1. To explore this further, we consider the case where both hosts have equal encounter rates and identify conditions under which the evolutionary singular point (σ∗ 

> h2

)

shifts, making the generalist strategy more stable as the trade-off strength χ increases. We found that the likelihood of vectors attacking h2 decreases when the trade-off between host specialization and feeding success weakens. Interventions such as insecticide-treated nets or repellents can reduce encounters between vectors and h1, thereby strengthening the trade-off and effectively increasing the relative encounter rate with h2. This shift lowers the evolutionary preference αv for h1, and ultimately helps reduce infection prevalence in that host. Our work raises a number of open questions for future work. From an eco-evolutionary per-spective, more detailed models may provide further insight on the effect of insecticide resistance in vectors or seasonal shifts in their populations on disease prevalence, which are not captured by our model. Incorporating real-world data on host and vector populations, vector feeding patterns, and intervention outcomes will allow more robust validation and refinement of model predictions. Our framework highlights the potential for adaptive vector behavior and host heterogeneity to influence disease dynamics, suggesting that interventions targeting a single host or ignoring vec-tor adaptation may have unintended consequences. With further development and integration of evolutionary and adaptive analyses, our modeling approach can help identify optimal strategies for vector control, guide public health decision-making, and improve the overall understanding of VBD dynamics across diverse ecological contexts.

The eigenvalue analysis reveals that all eigenvalues have negative real parts, confirming the local asymptotic stability of E∗. The phase portrait illustrates that all the trajectories originating within the region of attraction converge to the endemic equilibrium, further supporting the global stability of E∗.
