(Dated: March 17, 2026) The processes of morphogenesis that give rise to the shapes of organs and organisms during development are often driven by mechanical instabilities. Can such mechanical bifurcations also drive or constrain the evolution of these processes in the first place? We discover an instance of these constraints in the green algae of the family Volvocaceae. During their development, their bowl-shaped embryonic cell sheet turns itself inside out. This inversion is driven by a simple wave of cell wedging in the genus Pleodorina (16–128 cells) and more complex programmes of cell shape changes in Volvox (∼400–50 000 cells). However, no species with intermediate cell numbers (256 cells) have been described. Here, we relate this gap to a mechanical bifurcation: Focusing on the inversion of Pleodorina californica (64 cells), we develop a continuum model, in which the cell shape changes driving inversion appear as changes of the intrinsic curvature of an elastic surface. A mechanical bifurcation in this model predicts that inversion is only possible in a subset of its parameter space. Strikingly, parameters estimated for P. californica fall into this possible subset, but those that we extrapolate to 256 or more cells using allometric observations and a model of cell cleavage in Volvocaceae do not. Our work thus suggests that the more complex inversion strategies of Volvox are an evolutionary necessity to obviate this bifurcation and indicates more broadly how mechanical bifurcations can drive the evolution of morphogenesis. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Evolution has not only led to the shapes of tissues, or-gans, and organisms, but also to the morphogenetic processes through which these shapes arise during development. The emergence of these processes need not however be driven only by evolutionary pressures, but can also be driven by the physics and mechanics of morphogenesis. The cephalic furrow [1, 2], a tissue fold that appears transiently during Drosophila gastru-lation, provides a recent example of this: in mutants in which the cephalic furrow does not form, ectopic folds appear, sug-gesting that the cephalic furrow evolved as a mechanical means to absorb compressive stresses associated with germband ex-tension [1]. Interestingly, a different strategy to reduce com-pressive stresses (viz., out-of-plane cell divisions) has evolved in other fly species that lack a cephalic furrow [2]. Over the last decade or so, mechanical instabilities [3] have been recognised as drivers of the emergence of shapes in di-verse developmental processes [4–10]. This prompts the ques-tion: Can such mechanical bifurcations also constrain or limit the evolution of the mechanical processes of morphogenesis? Here, we discover an instance of these mechanical limits, by showing how a mechanical bifurcation constrains the evolution of cell sheet folding in the multicellular colonial algae of the family Volvocaceae that we introduce in detail below. The em-bryonic cell sheet of the Volvocaceae is bowl-shaped or spher-ical, and turns itself inside out in a process called inversion [11–13]. In the genus Pleodorina [16–128 cells, Fig. 1(a)], this inversion is driven by a wave of cell wedging [14], but more complex inversion processes [12] have evolved in the larger species of Volvox [∼400–50 000 cells, Fig. 1(a)]. Inversion in the family Volvocaceae 

The family Volvocaceae is a model for the evolution of multicellularity [16, 17] and, together with the closely related single-celled alga Chlamydomonas , has also become a model for biological fluid mechanics [18]. More recently, the Volvo-caceae have begun to emerge as models for cell sheet folding in development because of the fascinating process of inver-sion [11–13] by which the bowl-shaped or spherical embry-onic cell sheet turns itself inside out at the end of cell division. Through inversion, those cells poles whence will emanate cilia are exposed to the outside to enable motility. In the largest species, which are found in the genus Volvox ,inversion proceeds in at least two different, species-specific ways [12]: In type-A inversion [19–22], four lips flip over at the anterior pole of the cell sheet to pull it back and hence invert it [Fig. 1(a), inset]. In type-B inversion [15, 23–25], a circular invagination at the equator enables inversion of the posterior hemisphere; slightly later, an opening at the top of the cell sheet widens and the anterior hemisphere inverts by peeling over the inverted posterior [Fig. 1(a), inset]. > II. RESULTS A. Mechanical model of inversion in P. californica

We begin by setting up a mechanical model of inversion in 

P. californica , in which the cell sheet is an elastic sheet and the cell shape changes driving inversion appear as changes of the intrinsic, preferred geometry of this sheet. This model is in the spirit of previous mechanical models of inversion in Volvox [15, 22, 24, 25, 27]. The deformations of the cell sheet during inversion are approximately axisymmetric. We therefore describe the cell sheet by its undeformed arclength 𝑠 

from the posterior pole at 𝑠 = 0 [Fig. 1(b 1)]. The undeformed configuration of the cell sheet is a spherical cap of radius 𝑅 = 1

and thickness ℎ, with 𝑠 = 𝐿 corresponding to the rim of the cell sheet [Fig. 1(b 1)]. We denote by 𝜅 0 the intrinsic curvature of the cell sheet. Close to the posterior pole, the cells are spindle-shaped dur-ing inversion [Fig. 1(b 2)]. These spindle shapes, illustrated in Fig. 10(b) of Ref. [14], favour the uninverted curvature of the cell sheet, so that 𝜅 0 = 1 there. We emphasise that the iden-tically named spindle-shaped cells that appear during type-B inversion in V. globator , shown in Fig. 12(b) of Ref. [23], have different shapes: They are symmetric across the plane of cy-toplasmic bridges, which suggests that 𝜅 0 = 0 for those cells. This mechanical hypothesis has been verified in Ref. [25]. By contrast, 𝜅 0 = −𝑘 < 0 in the region of elongated cells be-hind the front of cell shape changes [Fig. 1(b 3)]. The fact that the radius of the cell sheet is smaller post-inversion than its pre-inversion radius [Fig. 1(b)] suggests that 𝑘 > 1. Indeed, the cell size measurements of Ref. [14] indicate that the cell shape changes of P. californica , unlike the cell shape changes of Volvox [15, 20, 22, 23], do not involve contraction or expan-sion within the plane of the cell sheet. A similar bifurcation has been implicated in Ref. [22] in the failure of type-A inversion under contractility perturbations and in mutants of V. carteri [26, 29]. We confirm this expectation by computing bifurcation di-agrams in (𝐵, E) space for different values of 𝑘 using the continuation package auto-07p [30], as described in detail in Appendix B. At low values of 𝑘 , the cell sheet does not invert on the branch of lowest energy that is connected to the initial, uninverted configuration of the cell sheet [Fig. 2(a)]. Inverted configurations of lower energy do exist in this bi-furcation diagram, but are not connected to the uninverted configuration [Fig. 2(a)]. At larger values of 𝑘 , this picture changes [Fig. 2(b)]: The cell sheet now inverts on the branch of lowest energy, while the branch on which the edge of the cell sheet does not flip over has disconnected from the uninverted configuration. These regimes are separated by a bifurcation at 𝑘 = 𝑘 1, where, as described in Appendix B, a pair of fold points emerges in the bifurcation diagram. There is in fact a second, relevant bifurcation in this model: At 𝑘 = 𝑘 2 > 𝑘 1, another pair of fold points merges at a branch point on the branch of lowest energy (Appendix B) so that inversion is continuous for 𝑘 > 𝑘 2 [Fig. 2(c)], while there is a snap-through at a fold point for 𝑘 1 < 𝑘 < 𝑘 2 as 𝑊 decreases [represented by the arrow in Fig. 2(b)]. It is the observation of merging fold points that enables numerical computation of 𝑘 2

in auto-07p (Appendix B). We thus compute the critical curvatures 𝑘 1, 𝑘 2 for different values of the remaining model parameters ℎ, 𝐿 . Thence we identify, in Fig. 3, the region in (ℎ, 𝐿, 𝑘 ) parameter space in which inversion is possible or continuous. It is bounded by the surfaces 𝑘 = 𝑘 1 (ℎ, 𝐿 ) or 𝑘 = 𝑘 2 (ℎ, 𝐿 ) and 𝑘 = 8/3ℎ, of which the latter corresponds to the limit of perfect cell constriction (Appendix A), and thus bounds the geometrically inaccessible region of parameter space in which the intrinsic cell shapes would self-intersect. Below this boundary sits the region of strong constriction in which the elastic shell theory underlying model is not formally valid (Appendix A).

F. Extrapolation of cell sheet geometries 

We were left to extrapolate the dimensionless extent of the cell sheet 𝐿 to species of more than 64 cells. This extent is set by the packing of the cells onto the spherical cap during the repeated rounds of cell division that the cell sheet undergoes before inversion [12, 70]. This packing in turn is constrained by the network of cytoplasmic bridges [21, 70]. The resulting cleavage patterns have been described in detail in different species of Volvox up to the 64-cell stage [70, 72–75]. A physics slant on these problems of cell packings has only emerged recently [76–79] through work highlighting their rela-tion to robustness of morphogenesis and the evolution of multi-cellularity. In particular, cell packings in the early Drosophila 

embryo [76] are constrained by cytoplasmic bridges, too. 

1. Combinatorial constraints on cell packings 

Observations of the cytoplasmic bridge system linking neighbouring cells of Volvox during the cleavage phase [21, 70] suggest the following rules: (1) if cell A divides into A1, A2,then A1, A2 are connected by cytoplasmic bridges (CBs); (2) if cells A, B are connected by CBs and divide into A1, A2, B1, B2,respectively, then each of A1, A2 is connected by CBs to at least one of B1, B2; (3) CBs connecting different pairs of cells to not cross. Consider three cells A, B, C, as shown in Fig. 6(a): cells 

A, B are at the phialopore, connected by CBs. Additionally, A

is connected to cell C, which is not at the phialopore. In a round of cell divisions, these cells divide into A1, A2, B1, B2, C1, C2,respectively. We claim that at most two of A1, A2, B1, B2 can remain at the phialopore. Indeed, first suppose to the contrary that A1, A2, B1, B2 are all at the phialopore [Fig. 6(a)]. By rule (1), A1, A2 and 

B1, B2 are connected by CBs, while, by rule (2) and without loss of generality, A1 is connected by CBs to B1. Hence, again 6without loss of generality, A2, A1, B1, B2 are in this order along the phialopore. Thus A2 neighbours neither B1 nor B2 (unless there are no other cells at the phialopore, 𝑁 1 = 4, which is not germane to our argument). This contradicts rule (2) and shows that at least one of A1, A2, B1, B2 is not at the phialopore. Thus suppose, again without loss of generality, that 

A2, A1, B1 are connected by CBs at the phialopore in this order, but that B2 is not [Fig. 6(a)]. By rule (1), B1, B2 are connected by CBs. Moreover, A2, B1 are not neighbours, so, unless 𝑁 1 = 3 (which is irrelevant to our argument), A2, B2

are connected by CBs by rule (2). However, rule (2) implies that A1 must be connected to one of C1, C2, which finally con-tradicts rule (3). [Indeed, since A1 is connected to by CBs to 

A2, B1 by assumption, this contradiction cannot be resolved by C1 or C2 being at the phialopore.] In this way, we have shown that at most two of A1, A2, B1, B2

can remain at the phialopore. (We note that this argument does not require cells B, C to be connected by CBs.) The phialopore thus leads to a combinatorial constraint on the cell packings: as the number of cells increases through repeated rounds of cell division, the number of cells at the phialopore cannot increase.  

> 2. Analysis of cell packings

We generate regular packings of 𝑁 ≈ 2𝑑 equally-sized cells into a spherical cap of size 𝐿 by extending the algorithm of Ref. [80], and denote by 𝑁 1 the number of cells surrounding the phialopore in each of these packings. We plot these packings in (𝑁, 𝑁 1, 𝐿 ) space in Fig. 6(b) for 𝑑 = 6–10. In particular, Fig. 6(b) classifies those packings consistent with the range of 𝐿 estimated in Table I for P. californica 

(𝑑 = 6). Packings with 𝑁 1 = 13 , consistent with Fig. 5(m) of Ref. [14], fall right into this range [Fig. 6(c)], although its lower end is also consistent with packings with 𝑁 1 = 17 . The combinatorial constraint proved above, that 𝑁 1 cannot increase if the sheet were to undergo further rounds of cell division, now translates to lower bounds on 𝐿 for these packings [Fig. 6(d)]. We note that these lower bounds increase with 𝑁 .This shows that, if 64-celled P. californica were to undergo further rounds of cell division, then 𝐿 would increase. Thus the phialopore would shrink compared to the size of the cell sheet and its shape would approximate the spherical one of Volvox .This increase of 𝐿 allowed us to analyse the possibility of inversion in such hypothetical individuals or species below. This increase of 𝐿 with 𝑁 is also consistent with that observed across the small genera of Volvocaceae [Fig. 6(c)]. We stress that this argument does not need to, and does not in fact reproduce the cleavage pattern of Volvox , for which 

𝐿 ≈ π throughout the cell division stage [70, 72, 75]. This cleavage pattern is not covered by our combinatorial argu-ment, because the cell shapes during early cleavage stages in 

Volvox are elongated [70, 72, 75]. The size difference between somatic and germ cells and the lips near the phialopore that occur in some, but not all species of Volvox [12] could also break this argument. However, already by the 64-cell stage, the cell packing of Volvox [70, 72, 75] does approximate the regular packings of Fig. 6(b), as does the cell packing of the smaller genera of Volvocaceae [12] and P. californica [14] in particular. This justifies the use of this model to extrapolate our measurements to cell packings resulting from 64-celled 

P. californica undergoing additional rounds of cleavage.

> III. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION

In this paper, we have discovered that a mechanical bifur-cation constrained the evolution of cell sheet folding in the family Volvocaceae: the simple programme of inversion in the genus Pleodorina [Fig. 1(b)] runs into a mechanical bifurca-tion; the more complex inversion programmes in the genus 

Volvox [Fig. 1(a), inset] are therefore, the model suggests, evo-lutionary innovations made necessary by this bifurcation. This indicates a new role of mechanical bifurcations in de-velopment, of constraining the evolution of morphogenetic processes. Future work will need to identify additional ex-amples of this role in different biological systems. This re-quires, in particular, quantitative mechanical models of these developmental processes. In the minimal model that is the development of Volvocaceae, this was enabled by an elas-tic model of inversion that has been tested extensively in 

Volvox [22, 24, 25, 27]. Still, it might be argued that the model is limited by the small amount of experimental data underlying the quantification of the model parameters (Appendix C). It is therefore fortunate that the underlying bifurcation (Fig. 7) is “unavoidable” in parameter space as long as the qualitative behaviour implied by our quantitative estimates continues to hold: the cell sheet becomes (relatively) thinner and more spherical as the number of cells increases. More physically, our results have highlighted the role of apical constriction in overcoming the mechanical bifurcation through cell shape changes that impart more intrinsic curva-ture to the cell sheet. Our mechanical model (Appendix A) has stressed the role of the mechanics of constriction: in the limit of large intrinsic curvatures associated with strongly constricted cells, the mechanics of the cell sheet change quantitatively and even qualitatively [28]. Still, true constriction is a geometric singularity that displays interesting mechanical consequences even in minimal models [81]. Obtaining a more general con-tinuum theory of cell constriction in tissues therefore remains an interesting avenue for future theoretical work.

> APPENDIX B: BIFURCATION NUMERICS

To determine the critical curvatures 𝑘 1 and 𝑘 2 numerically, we computed branches of fold points in (𝑊, 𝑘 ) space (Fig. 9) by two-parameter continuation in auto-07p [30] for fixed values of the remaining model parameters ℎ, 𝐿 .At intermediate values of 𝑘 > 𝑘 1, three such fold points appear in the (𝑊, E) diagram (Fig. 9, inset): two fold points on the branch A connected to the undeformed configuration, and another fold point on the branch B, not connected to A, that contains uninverted solutions at 𝑊 = 0 [Fig. 2(b)]. We denote these fold points by LP 1, LP 2, LP 3 in increasing order of the corresponding value of 𝑊 . Branch A has, moreover, a branch point between LP 2 and LP 3 at which it connects to a branch C of solutions of higher energy that are not relevant physically. Thus LP 1 and LP 2 merge at the local minimum of the branch of fold points in Fig. 9. Hence this minimum defines 𝑘 = 𝑘 1.For 𝑘 < 𝑘 1, but a single fold point remains; this corresponds to the fold on the branch in Fig. 2(a) containing the inverted solution at 𝑊 = 0. Similarly, LP 2 and LP 3 merge with the branch point mentioned above at the local maximum of this branch of fold points (Fig. 9). This local maximum is a cusp, and it defines 𝑘 = 𝑘 2.Additionally, LP 1 disappears at 𝑘 = 𝑘 3 > 𝑘 1. At this value, branch B disappears. In other words, the uninverted shape with 𝑊 = 0 is not a solution at larger 𝑘 .
