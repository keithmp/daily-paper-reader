# Sequential learning theory for Markov genealogy processes 

## David J. Pascall 11MRC Biostatistics Unit, University of Cambridge, Cambridge, UK 

# 1 Abstract 

We introduce a filtration-based framework for studying when and why adding taxa improves phylodynamic inference, by constructing a natural ordering of observed tips and applying sequential Bayesian analysis to the resulting filtration. We decompose the expected variance reduction on taxa addition into learning, mismatch, and covariance components, classify estimands into learning classes based on the pathwise behaviour of the mismatch, and show that for absorbing estimands an oracle who knows the latent absorption status obtains event-wise learning guarantees unavailable to the analyst. The gap between oracle and analyst is irreducible under stochastic sampling processes, establishing a fundamental limit on what sequence data alone can reveal about the latent genealogy. 

# 2 Introduction 

A natural question in phylodynamic inference is whether adding taxa to an analysis always improves esti-mation. Practitioners have observed that this is not always the case, as additional sequences can increase posterior uncertainty, degrade mixing, or amplify model misspecification. Yet the theoretical foundations for understanding when and why taxa addition helps or hurts are largely absent. In this paper, we introduce a filtration-based framework for studying this question. By constructing a random ordering of observed tips via a uniform permutation, we obtain a natural filtration on the sequence data, which allows standard results from sequential Bayesian analysis to be applied to phylodynamic inference. The framework reveals that the answer depends critically on what is being estimated: for fixed parameters like substitution or birth rates, classical guarantees apply directly, but for estimands that change with the sample (such as the tMRCA of the included tips) the situation is more complex, because the target itself shifts as taxa are added. 1

We show that for any sequential estimand with a well-defined limit target (the value that would be obtained if the entire latent genealogy were observed), the expected posterior variance about that limit target decreases on taxa addition. However, this process-averaged guarantee masks important structure. We decompose the variance change into learning, mismatch, and covariance components. We also show that for absorbing estimands, those sequential estimands where there is a possibility of early maintained equality with the limit estimand, the learning process simplifies dramatically post-absorption. We introduce an oracle who knows the absorption status and show that the oracle obtains event-wise learning guarantees that the analyst cannot access, because the absorption status depends on the latent genealogy. The gap between oracle and analyst is irreducible: even after all sampled tips are observed, the analyst’s uncertainty strictly exceeds the oracle’s, reflecting a fundamental limit on what can be learned from sequence data alone without knowledge of the latent process structure.

# 4 Results 

The first thing that the setup above allows us to do is classify types of estimands into what we call learn-ing classes . Learning classes aim for a full classification of sequential estimands in MGPs by relating the permutation-variant estimands in the sequence to their limit estimand, K∞. Sequential estimands are first divided by whether the mismatch path is monotone. They are then divided by whether equality with the 3limit estimand is reachable before nmax with positive probability and if it is, once reached, is absorbing in all, some or no cases. Constant sequences are then separated into their own class of degenerate absorbing monotone estimands. 

• Fixed: Constant sequential estimands. Kn = Kf a.s. for all n.

This relates the previous result to learning, and applies generally across all the learning classes. We already observed that the uncertainty about the limit target decreased on average on taxa addition, but this gives us a mechanistic breakdown of where that learning is occurring. The first pair of terms is analogous to a classical learning term, the expected change in our uncertainty about the current target in the sequence on taxa addition. The second pair is a "mismatch" term, measuring the expected change in our uncertainty 5about how far the current target is from the limit estimand. The final term is about the expected change in how the uncertainty about the current target and the uncertainty about the discrepancy covary. None of the three terms have an obvious sign; adding a taxon may increase uncertainty about the new sequential target, the uncertainty about the distance of the current target depends on the learning class of the estimand (learning classes are precisely about the pathwise structure of this mismatch) but remain hard to conceptualise in general, and the covariance structure relies on the precise geometry of phylogenetic posteriors which are complex and poorly understood. Despite this, the theorem says that while these three terms can trade-off against each other, their sum must remain non-negative by the improvement guarantees of Proposition 1. For absorbing monotonic or absorbing learning class estimands, we can get a further decomposition in terms of the absorbing event, which allows further characterisation of the learning for these classes.

That is, even after observing all sampled tips, the analyst’s posterior variance strictly exceeds the expected oracle posterior variance, assuming terminal sequential mean-independence from absorption status. In other words, that knowing whether your current sequential target already equals the limit target shouldn’t impact your expected estimate of the current target. This is very natural assumption for many phylogenetic estimands. Consider the tMRCA of all the tips in the current sample. We introduce the straddling criterion to talk about this geometrically. Consider the root of a tree, a set of tips straddles the root if the set contains a tip from at least two distinct subtrees descending from it. Straddling can be defined for all other nodes similarly. As soon as a set of tips straddles the root, the tMRCA of that set of tips is fixed to the root age. Straddling for non-root nodes only implies that the tMRCA of that set is at least as old as the age of that node, but it is easy to introduce a notion of strict straddling , which adds the condition that the set does not contain any tips that don’t descend from that node, which carries the properties at the root through to non-root nodes. Knowing whether the current sample has straddled the root should not change the estimate of the tMRCA of the current sample set, but it should still impact your estimate of the age of the root of the full latent genealogy, the terminal sequential mean-independence from absorption status assumption encodes precisely this intuition.
