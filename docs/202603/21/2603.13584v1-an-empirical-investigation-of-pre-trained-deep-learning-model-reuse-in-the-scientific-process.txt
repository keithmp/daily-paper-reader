## Abstract 

Deep learning has achieved recognition for its impact within nat-ural sciences, however scientists are inhibited by the prohibitive technical cost and computational complexity of training project specific models from scratch. Following software engineering com-munity guidance, natural scientists are reusing pre-trained deep learning models (PTMs) to amortize these costs. While prior works recommend PTM reuse patterns, to our knowledge, little work has been done to empirically evaluate their usage and impact within the natural sciences. We present the first empirical study of PTM reuse patterns in the natural sciences, quantifying the utilization and impact of concep-tual, adaptation, and deployment reuse within the scientific process. Leveraging an automated large language model driven pipeline, we analyze 17,511 peer reviewed, open access papers to identify PTM reuse by scientific field, associated reuse patterns, and the impact of PTM integration into the scientific process from January 1st, 2000 to December 10th, 2025. Our results show that “Biochemistry, Genetics and Molecular Biology” has outpaced other natural scien-tific fields in PTM reuse, “adaptation” reuse is the most prevalent PTM reuse pattern identified across all natural science fields, and the “Test” stage of the scientific process has been most impacted by PTM integration. This aligns with the growing interest of lever-aging computational methods to conduct high throughput, data driven scientific research. Our work characterizes and identifies current PTM reuse practices within the natural sciences, evaluates their impact on the scientific process, and establishes a founda-tion for future work into the implementation and broader scientific implications of PTM reuse.

## 1 Introduction 

The 2024 Nobel Prizes in Physics [ 86 ] and Chemistry [ 85 ] recog-nized the significance of Deep learning (DL), honoring foundational neural networks [ 32 ] and their transformative application in pro-tein structure prediction [ 41 ]. DL models have enabled scientists to extract insights from and model relationships inherent in natural systems [ 46 ]. These data driven models frequently match or exceed the performance of traditional, hand-crafted methods [ 5 ]. These developments confirm that DL is playing an increasingly central role in modern natural science research [19]. While DL offers a transformative opportunity for the natural sciences, the prohibitive technical cost and computational complex-ity inhibit the ability to create models per project. Pre-trained DL models (PTMs) have been identified as a solution to realize the potential of data driven scientific discovery by effectively amor-tizing the costs associated with DL training [ 16 , 28 , 37 ]. This is supported by an expanding ecosystem of frameworks [ 3, 7, 12 ], fine-tuning algorithms [ 69 ], and model registries that facilitate

programmatic access to PTMs [ 1 , 2 ]. But due to algorithmic com-plexity [ 82 ], strong dependence on data quality [ 76 ], and challenges associated with deploying and maintaining models [ 55 ] access to this ecosystem is prohibitive. Prior works have proposed qualitative strategies to remedy these issues including identifying effective software engineering practices for scientific software [ 14 ], and de-veloping domain specific strategies for utilizing DL [ 15 , 46 , 53 , 81 ]. However, there remains a knowledge gap in assessing PTM reuse within science. To our knowledge, no prior work has empirically quantified the prominence and impact of PTM reuse patterns ( i.e. ,conceptual, adaptation, and deployment) [ 16 , 37 ] within the natural sciences. To address this, we conduct the first empirical investigation of PTM reuse within the natural sciences. Leveraging an automated, large language model (LLM) driven pipeline to analyze 17,511 peer reviewed, open access papers from four prominent mega journals, we construct a dataset mapping natural science works ( i.e. , biology, chemistry, physics, and environmental science) to the PTMs reused, the applied PTM reuse pattern, and the stage within the scientific process the PTM impacted. Out of the 17,511 papers analyzed, we identified 631 papers reusing PTMs. Within this subset, researchers in “Biochemistry, Genetics and Molecular Biology” emerged as the most frequent adopters. The “adaptation” pattern was the predom-inant mode of reuse, while our analysis of the scientific process revealed that the “Test” stage experienced the most significant im-pact from PTM integration. This work provides the first quantitative study of PTM reuse within the natural sciences. Our primary contributions are (1) a systematic identification of PTM reuse patterns via an automated, LLM-driven pipeline, (2) an empirical characterization of how these models augment the scientific process to enhance research pro-ductivity, and (3) the release of a comprehensive dataset mapping scientific literature to specific PTM reuse patterns and their impact within the scientific process.

Our findings establish a reproducible methodology for the longitudinal study of AI in science and provide the community with a quantitative baseline to guide the development of future engineering tools for scientific model reuse. Furthermore, our work identifies areas where PTMs are currently underutilized within the scientific process, thereby providing the foundational evidence needed to support future works on the integration of PTMs in under served areas.

## 2 Background and Related Work 

Pre-trained deep learning model (PTM) reuse in natural science draws on established software engineering community reuse pat-terns and their specialized integrations into the scientific process. This section first reviews the principles of existing PTM reuse pat-terns (Section 2.1). We then examine specific applications of PTMs within natural science research (Section 2.2). 

## 2.1 Pre-Trained Model Reuse 

Deep learning (DL) models are emerging as an increasingly impor-tant dependency in software systems [ 38 , 39 , 82 ]. However, training these models incurs significant technical [ 76 ], financial [ 75 ], and environmental [ 75 , 79 ] costs. To mitigate these, engineers are en-couraged to leverage PTMs as they encapsulate DL model’s architec-ture and learned weights into a distributable software package [ 16 ]. The dissemination of these artifacts is facilitated by an ecosystem PTM registries [ 2 , 95 ], and specialized libraries [ 7, 91 ], which serve as hosting infrastructure and provide programmatic interfaces for model management and integration. Within this ecosystem, PTM reuse implementations can be categorized into distinct PTM reuse patterns listed in Table 1. While prior work has surveyed practitioners to understand PTM applications and challenges [ 36 , 37 ], current empirical efforts re-main focused on general software systems. These include investi-gations into the performance and portability implications of con-ceptual reuse [ 36 ], taxonomies of adaptation techniques [ 69 ], and the technical difficulties inherent in deployment reuse [ 34 ]. To our knowledge, this work provides the first empirical study of PTM reuse leveraged within the natural sciences, addressing a significant gap in our understanding of the PTM reuse practices. ## 2.2 Pre-Trained Models in Science 

DL methods are being increasingly leveraged in the natural sci-ences to enable data driven discoveries [ 6, 10 , 19 , 46 , 53 , 71 , 81 ]. To facilitate the democratization of these techniques, current rec-ommendations favor the refinement of existing PTMs on domain specific data over the costly development of bespoke models for individual projects [ 47 ]. In practice, deep learning methods have been leveraged to assist in molecular protein design [ 41 , 43 , 96 ], understanding chemical reactions [ 74 , 99 ], and modeling seismic events [ 58 , 89 ]. However, to our knowledge, little work has evalu-ated how PTMs are being integrated into scientific processes. Our work contributes to the formalization of PTM reuse within the natural sciences by empirically evaluating established PTM reuse patterns and their impact on the scientific process. By map-ping how PTMs are integrated into this process, we provide a frame-work for assessing their impact on discovery, ultimately informing the development of more robust and reproducible software ecosys-tems. Our work extends existing software engineering foundations by characterizing how scientists employ conceptual, adaptation, and deployment PTM reuse patterns within specific research stages, identifying both prevalent practices and underused opportunities for future integration. 

Agentic Reuse in Science We do recognize an emerging agentic 

reuse pattern that augments the scientific process by automating complex tasks including parsing academic literature, generating hypotheses, conducting experiments, and synthesizing reports [ 24 ,31 , 50 , 94 ]. The agentic reuse pattern redefines scientific inquiry through deep human-AI collaboration and is increasingly central to the operations of autonomous laboratories [ 4 , 21 , 40 ]. However, we explicitly exclude agentic reuse from this study to focus on the modular integration of PTMs as tools for discrete scientific tasks as this pattern is still nascent.

4.1.1 Extraction of Candidate Papers From Mega-Journals We char-acterize PTM reuse patterns in natural science by constructing a dataset of peer reviewed, open access publications. Existing method-ologies for mapping research publications to software rely on trac-ing citations from known software artifacts [ 52 ]. However, because scientific software is frequently under cited [ 33 ], a citation centric approach risks omitting a significant volume of relevant work. To mitigate this and ensure the completeness of our dataset, we employ a search strategy where we first query open access mega-journals for DL keywords and then normalize the resulting paper metadata with OpenAlex [68]. Our empirical study analyzes peer reviewed, open access papers published between January 1st, 2000 and December 10th, 2025. We target the BMJ, F1000, FrontiersIn, and PLOS mega-journals to serve as representative proxies for broad scientific discourse [ 11 ]. We first queried each of these journals using the following keywords: “Deep Learning ”, “ Deep Neural Network ”, “ Model Weights ”, “ Hugging Face ” [ 37 ], “ Model Checkpoint ” [ 38 ], and “ Pre-Trained Model ” [ 16 ]. 4.1.2 Filtering and Transformation of Natural Science Papers With the set of 17,479 OpenAlex indexed candidate papers, we then proceed to first filter for papers with citations followed by those classified as natural science works, then transform the prose of each natural science work into Markdown for LLM analysis. We filter for papers that OpenAlex reports to have been cited at least once to ensure that our study is focused on works that have made spe-cific impacts in their respective fields. Our filtering on this criteria resulted in 13,815 papers with citations. We then filter on the OpenAlex topic fields for each candidate work to identify natural science works. OpenAlex independently classifies papers into 26 distinct topic fields and returns the top three most likely fields for a given work [ 61 ]. However, it does not provide an explicit “natural science” classification. To remedy this, we classified existing OpenAlex topic fields as either “natural sci-ence” or “non-natural science” according to the definition provided by [ 22 ]. Two authors independently audited the OpenAlex topic fields and engaged in inter-author agreement until total consensus was met on the topic field classification. This resulted in a list of seven identified natural science topic fields, presented in Table 2. To isolate natural science research, we implemented a majority vote filtering process requiring at least two identified natural sci-ence topic fields per paper resulting in 4,384 papers. This threshold prioritizes works with high thematic relevance while ensuring our dataset maintains a primary focus on the natural sciences. Our majority vote filtering process on OpenAlex topic fields retains relevant interdisciplinary works while filtering out those where the Natural Sciences are only a peripheral focus. For each candidate natural science work, prose was retrieved in Journal Attribution Tag Suite (JATS) XML format [ 60 ]. 4.1.3 Automated Analysis of Filtered Papers To characterize the longitudinal shifts in PTM reuse, we developed an automated ex-traction pipeline to analyze the candidate natural science works. For each publication, we address a four-tier hierarchy of inquiries: (1) identification of DL methods, (2) verification of PTM reuse, (3) extraction of specific PTM identifiers, and (4) classification of the associated reuse pattern ( i.e. , conceptual, adaptation, and deploy-ment). Questions (1) and (2) function as exclusionary filters whereby papers failing to meet these criteria are removed from subsequent analysis. We utilized CO-STAR formatted system prompts [ 84 ] to enable GPT-OSS 120:b [ 62 ] to analyze each paper. To guide system prompt development, we randomly selected 10 papers per year from 2015 to 2025 (110 total papers) and validated the LLM-generated responses against manual annotations for each of the four hierarchical ques-tions. To enhance the recognition of PTM names, we augmented our prompts with a dictionary of known architectures from the Hugging Face Transformers [ 92 ] and Diffusers [ 88 ] libraries for questions (2), (3) and (4). Prompts were modified until agreement reached 90%. Our Prompt Example presents an example system prompt.

## 4.2 Implementation 

We utilized the GPT-OSS 120:b model [ 62 ] hosted on the Argonne National Laboratory Sophia Inference Server API endpoint 1 to an-swer these questions. To mitigate the inherent non-determinism of large language models and ensure experimental reproducibility, we implemented a static seed value of 42. The model’s sampling behavior was constrained setting both the temperature and top-p parameters to 0.1. Furthermore, the model was configured with a “high” reasoning effort setting to prioritize complex logical infer-ence, while the frequency penalty was maintained at 0 to avoid artificial suppression of repeated tokens in the output. All responses were requested in a structured JSON.

## 5 RQ2: PTM Integration In Scientific Processes 

This section details our methodology for identifying what stages of the scientific process are being augmented via PTM reuse within natural science between January 1st, 2000 and December 10th, 2025. Section 5.1.1 and Section 4.1.2 describe our methodology for isolating paper sections implicating the reuse of PTMs and our strategy for classifying these sections as one of the stages of the scientific process. Section 5.2 reports our findings on how the scientific process has been augmented with PTMs within natural science. 

## 5.1 Methods 

To identify how PTM reuse has augmented the scientific process within natural sciences, we developed a pipeline designed to transi-tion from specific identification of PTMs to the greater context in which they are reused. First, we isolate sections of the prose specif-ically mentioning PTMs as identified from our previous analysis (Section 5.1.1). We subsequently employ a large language model to map these isolated prose segments against a formalized taxonomy of the scientific process (Section 5.1.2). This allows us to categorize the specific functional role of each PTM utilization instance and de-termine which research phase is being augmented by model reuse. We report the methodology for each step in this section.

While our previous methods to extract PTM model names and reuse patterns returned the most salient prose to confirm the output, it did not return the section in which that prose is located in. To remedy this, we parse the Markdown version of the document for the PTM name, and return a set of all sections of the prose that reference the model. 

5.1.2 Classification of Augmented Scientific Process To auto-matically classify the stages of the scientific process have been augmented by PTM reuse, we leverage the GPT-OSS 120:b large language model with the same parameters outlined in Section 4.1.3. ## 6 Discussion 

This study examines how natural scientists integrate pre-trained deep learning models (PTMs) into their scientific workflows to facilitate data driven discovery. We present a qualitative analysis of 17,511 peer reviewed, open access papers in biology, chemistry, physics, and environmental science published in BMJ, F1000, Fron-tiersIn, and PLOS since January 1st, 2000. This dataset serves as a proxy for evaluating the current landscape of PTM reuse across the scientific community. 

## 6.1 Implications For Science 

PTM Supply Chain Enabling “Adaptation” Reuse The findings in Section 4 suggest a few notable patterns regarding PTM adop-tion in the natural sciences. First, there is a visible increase in deep learning (DL) and PTM reuse, particularly within “Biochemistry, Genetics and Molecular Biology” (Figures 4 and 5). Second, the data shows that the “adaptation” pattern is used more frequently than “deployment” or “conceptual” reuse (Figure 6). By utilizing “adapta-tion”, researchers are able to lower the technical and computational barriers typically associated with DL methods. Given the prevalence of the PTM “adaptation” reuse pattern, we posit that scientists leverage this approach to realize the benefits of DL methodologies without incurring the prohibitive financial and computational costs of training models from scratch. In this capac-ity, PTMs serve as reusable scientific infrastructure that minimizes the need for domain specific specialization. The broader impact is a shift away from training DL models per project, towards the greater integration of existing PTM supply chains into the natural sciences. 

Limited PTM Integration in Scientific Processes As detailed in Section 5, PTM integration is concentrated within the “Test” stage of the scientific process (Figure 7). This concentration suggests that PTMs are currently utilized as evaluative or benchmarking instru-ments rather than as foundational components of early stage ex-perimental design. Furthermore, the limited reuse of PTMs in other An Empirical Investigation of Pre-Trained Deep Learning Model Reuse in the Scientific Process 

stages of the scientific process implies the existence of persistent integration barriers, highlighting a potential missed opportunity to leverage PTMs earlier in the scientific process 2.To address this missed opportunity, future research must pri-oritize the expansion of PTM utility across the broader scientific process. By limiting PTM usage to data evaluation, researchers may be overlooking a significant opportunity to leverage these models during the formative stages of inquiry, such as hypothesis genera-tion or experimental modeling. By shifting the focus toward these earlier stages, the scientific community can move toward a more holistic integration of PTMs that accelerates the entire hypothetico-deductive process. 

## 6.2 Future Directions 

Expanding Beyond Natural Science We plan to expand the scope of analysis to additional academic disciplines. By examining works from a broader range of fields, we can assess the current state of practice regarding PTM models and reuse in science more gener-ally. This large scale analysis will allow us to determine whether common PTM reuse patterns emerge across disciplines. 

Exploring PTM Integration into the Scientific Process Future work should investigate the technical implementation of PTM reuse patterns within scientific software to determine their influence on the scientific process. By analyzing how researchers integrate PTM packages into their code, we can discern whether these models are utilized as drivers of scientific reasoning or merely as tools for data automation. Delineating these software engineering practices is essential for identifying the process level frameworks that enable effective, reproducible model reuse across scientific domains. 

Research Into Agentic Scientific Workflows This paper does not evaluate the prevalence of agentic scientific workflows, as they represent an emerging form of PTM reuse. However, scientific practice is becoming progressively automated through tools such as autonomous laboratories and large language models for hy-pothesis generation and experimental design. Understanding how researchers integrate agents into their workflows will be critical for assessing their impact on scientific outcomes. Future research should examine how agentic systems are embedded within domain specific workflows and how such integration reshapes experimental design, validation, and knowledge production. 

## 7 Threats To Validity 

Construct Threats Construct threats are those that affect the measurements of our study. First, we rely on OpenAlex generated topic field assignments per paper to identify natural science papers. As these fields are inferred using deep learning, misclassifications may affect our selection of papers for our dataset. Second, our research is subject to reporting bias as researchers may not disclose PTM reuse in their works, thereby limiting our ability to identify PTM reuse. Additionally, due to the how researchers describe their methods, it is possible that we were unable to capture every PTM reuse and deep learning usage candidate from the academic prose.  

> 2It is also possible that PTMs are used elsewhere in the process but not disclosed, e.g ,through undocumented use of large language models (LLMs) in study design.

Third, we leveraged GPT-OSS 120:b to analyze prose for our analysis. GPT-OSS 120:b is limited in its reasoning capacity compared to current commercial offerings from frontier labs, but was selected to enable reproducibility in our analysis methodology. 

Internal Threats Internal threats to validity are those that effect the cause-effect relationships between variables. First, while we posit that the increasing reuse of PTMs in natural science is tied to the increasing usage of deep learning methods, we acknowledge that the reverse could hold true. PTM reuse could be driving the increasing deep learning usage we measured. Second, the increas-ing deep learning usage and PTM reuse that we measured could be due to several factors. These include the increasing availability of GPU and supercomputing resources, lower financial and technical barriers for reusing PTMs, and better library support and matura-tion of the PTM supply chain. Third, our usage of GPT-OSS 120:b to analyze natural science works may be inhibited due to the stochas-ticity of the large language model. To mitigate this, we specified parameters and seed values in Section 4.2. However, the potential for misclassifications and hallucinations is present in our analysis. 

External Threats External threats are those that affect the gen-eralization of our study. First, our study is limited to a selection of four open-access mega-journals. PTM reuse patterns measured in these venues may not reflect the patterns in other academic journals. Second, our analysis is focused on PTM reuse within the fields of biology, chemistry, physics, and environmental science. As such, the results of our study may not generalize well to other scientific disciplines. Third, our study relies on the analysis of aca-demic works available in the Journal Article Tag Suite (JATS) XML format returned using a keyword search strategy. As such, potential candidate works may have fallen outside of our search criteria due to not supporting a JATS XML formatted version of the document or not being captured by our keyword search, thereby constraining our pool of candidate papers for this study. 

## 8 Conclusion 

To our knowledge, this work presents the first empirical study of pre-trained deep learning model (PTM) reuse patterns within the natural sciences. By examining how scientists reuse PTMs and re-port how they reused them, we characterize the impact that PTM reuse patterns have made on the scientific process. Our findings highlight the increasing adoption of PTM reuse to enable data-driven scientific discoveries, the overwhelming usage of the “adap-tation” reuse pattern to enable PTM reuse, and that the primary in-tegration of PTMs within the scientific process has occurred within the “Test” stage. Collectively, these findings highlight a maturing pattern of PTM integration within the natural sciences charac-terized by adaptation focused reuse and downstream evaluative application.
