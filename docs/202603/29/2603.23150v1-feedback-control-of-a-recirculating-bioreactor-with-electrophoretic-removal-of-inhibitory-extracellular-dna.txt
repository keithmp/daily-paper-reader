# Feedback Control of a Recirculating Bioreactor with Electrophoretic Removal of Inhibitory Extracellular DNA

Abstract. Extracellular DNA accumulation in recirculating bio-processes inhibits microbial growth and reduces productivity. We consider a continuous bioreactor with a recirculating loop and an electrophoretic filtration unit for selective DNA removal, and develop a feedback control framework combining online state and parameter estimation via an Unscented Kalman Filter with two control strategies: an adaptive Model Predictive Controller that jointly optimizes dilution rate and filtration activation, and a simpler bang–bang filtration policy with lookup-table dilution rate selection. Closed-loop simulations under nominal and per-turbed conditions show that the MPC strategy achieves signifi-cantly higher cumulative profit while keeping DNA concentration below the inhibition threshold. 

## 1 Introduction 

Continuous bioprocesses based on chemostat operation are widely adopted in biomanufacturing because they enable near steady-state operation with stable productivity and product qual-ity [1, 2]. However, classical chemostat operation requires a con-tinuous supply of fresh medium and a continuous discharge of culture broth. Increasingly strict environmental regulations and circular-economy policies have pushed industrial bioprocesses to-ward reduced-discharge or recirculating configurations [3, 4, 5]. While recirculation can significantly reduce water consumption and effluent generation, it may also cause the accumulation of in-hibitory compounds in the culture medium, leading to reduced productivity [6, 7]. In particular, extracellular DNA released during microbial growth has recently been identified as a potent inhibitor of cell proliferation when its concentration exceeds a critical threshold [8, 9]. To mitigate this effect, we consider the integration of an electrophoretic filtration module that exploits the strong electric charge of DNA molecules to selectively remove them from the recirculating stream [10]. Although the filtration device is currently under experimental validation, in this work we assume its availability and focus on the design of a control frame-work that jointly regulates the dilution rate and the activation of the filtration unit to maximize the economic performance of the bioprocess. We develop two feedback control strategies and compare their performance. The first combines online state and parameter esti-mation via an Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) with an adaptive Model Predictive Controller (MPC) that optimizes the control inputs over a receding horizon. The second is a simpler policy pairing bang–bang regulation of the filtration unit with a lookup-table selection of the dilution rate, also driven by UKF estimates. Both strategies are validated through closed-loop simulations of the nonlinear process model under nominal and perturbed param-eter conditions, and compared in terms of cumulative economic profit. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, this is the first work to address the feedback control of a bioreactor in which extracellular DNA accumulation acts as a growth inhibitor and is actively re-moved through electrophoretic filtration. While model predictive control of bioreactors has been extensively studied in the litera-ture [11], and the inhibitory role of extracellular DNA has been recently characterized experimentally [8, 9], the integration of these two aspects into a unified estimation and control framework is novel. In particular, the joint online estimation of states and kinetic parameters enables the adaptive controller to compensate for the significant parametric uncertainty that characterizes bio-logical systems, without requiring accurate prior knowledge of the growth kinetics.

2.1 Parameter identification 

The kinetic parameters were identified from experimental datasets describing aerobic growth of Saccharomyces cerevisiae in fed-batch conditions, reported in [8, 14]. Since the available data correspond to fed-batch operation, the model (2.1) was reduced to its classical chemostat formulation by setting 𝐷 rec = 0 and 

𝛿 = 0. Parameter estimation was performed by nonlinear least squares, minimizing the discrepancy between model predictions and experimental measurements of biomass, substrate, and ex-tracellular DNA concentrations. The resulting parameter values and their confidence intervals are summarized in Table 1, and the fitting results are shown in Fig. 2. The inhibition threshold 𝑥 crit was obtained from prior experi-ments on extracellular DNA effects in microbial systems, while the filtration efficiency 𝛼 was estimated from preliminary tests on a prototype of the electrophoretic filter. We remark that the confidence intervals in Table 1 reflect the precision of the least-squares fit. In practice, biological systems exhibit significantly larger variability due to strain differences and environmental con-ditions; this motivates the ±15% perturbation range adopted in the robustness analysis of Section 5.

## 4 Control Design 

To address the control problem formulated in Section 2.2, we design a feedback control architecture that jointly regulates the dilution rate 𝐷 and the activation of the electrophoretic filtration unit 𝛿 , based on the state and parameter estimates provided online by the UKF described in Section 3. Two strategies are considered: an adaptive Model Predictive Controller (UKF+MPC), which op-timizes the control inputs online using the current parameter esti-mates, and a simpler policy (UKF+lookup) combining bang-bang regulation of the filtration unit with a lookup-table selection of the dilution rate computed offline. The latter serves as a com-putationally inexpensive baseline that does not require solving an optimization problem at each sampling instant, making it suit-able for applications where real-time computational resources are limited.

## 5 Simulation results 

The two control strategies are compared through closed-loop sim-ulations of the nonlinear process model (2.1). The evaluation proceeds in two stages: first the controllers are validated under nominal parameter conditions, then under parametric uncertainty obtained by perturbing the kinetic parameters within ±15% of their nominal values. In both cases, the comparison focuses on the dynamic evolution of the process states and on the cumulative economic profit (2.7). Both controllers operate with a sampling time of 𝑇 𝑠 = 0.75 h. For the MPC strategy, the prediction horizon is set to 𝑁 = 10 steps, corresponding to 7.5 h. 

5.1 Nominal closed-loop performance 

We first compare the two strategies when the plant parameters coincide with their nominal values, simulating the process in closed-loop over a horizon of 30 h. Figure 3a shows the evo-lution of the main process variables. Both strategies maintain the DNA concentration well below the critical threshold 𝑥 crit , but their transient behaviour differs significantly. The UKF+MPC controller drives the biomass to its steady-state value in approxi-mately 10 h, reaching the high-productivity operating region sub-stantially faster than the lookup-based controller, which is con-strained by its offline-computed dilution rate. The corresponding control inputs are shown in Fig. 3b. The lookup controller applies a nearly constant dilution rate from the precomputed table, activating the filtration unit only when the DNA concentration approaches the upper hysteresis threshold. In contrast, the MPC continuously adapts both the dilution rate and the filter activation based on the current state and parame-ter estimates, resulting in a more dynamic control profile with frequent adjustments. The effect of these different control policies is reflected in the economic performance reported in Fig. 3c. The cumulative profit obtained with the adaptive UKF+MPC controller is signifi-cantly higher than that achieved by the lookup-based strategy. In particular, the final cumulative gain achieved by the UKF+MPC controller is 26 .7, while the UKF+lookup strategy reaches 17 .0.This corresponds to an improvement of approximately 36% in economic performance.

## 6 Conclusions 

We addressed the feedback control of a continuous bioreactor in which extracellular DNA accumulates in the recirculating medium and inhibits microbial growth. An electrophoretic filtra-tion unit integrated into the recirculation loop selectively removes DNA, and two control strategies were developed: an adaptive UKF+MPC scheme and a simpler UKF+lookup policy combin-ing bang–bang filtration with offline dilution rate selection. Both strategies kept DNA below the critical inhibition threshold, but the MPC consistently achieved higher cumulative profit through more efficient transient operation and more aggressive use of filtration. The lookup-based controller remains viable when real-time computational resources are limited. Future work will target experimental validation on a laboratory-scale bioreactor and ex-tension of the MPC formulation to include additional constraints such as nutrient consumption limits and minimum biomass pro-ductivity guarantees.
