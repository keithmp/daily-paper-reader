THE INTERPLAY OF SELECTION AND DORMANCY IN A MORAN MODEL CAN LEAD TO COEXISTENCE OF TYPES 

Jochen Blath 1, Baptiste Le Duigou 2 and András Tóbiás 3

(March 2, 2026) 

Abstract: In this paper we propose a Moran model that describes the population dynamics of two types: While the first type has a selective advantage during reproduc-tion, the second type can avoid replacement during reproduction with some positive probability by switching temporarily into a dormant state .We investigate the interplay of both evolutionary strategies by studying the invasion dynamics of the dormant type into the resident (selectively advantageous) population in the large population limit of the system. It turns out that the dormancy trait can not only invade and subsequently fixate under suitable parameter assumptions despite its selective disadvantage (a phenomenon that has already been observed in a related context in Blath and Tóbiás [BT20]), but that there is also a novel regime of stable coexistence of both types due to a frequency-dependent balancing effect that did not arise in the previous setup with Lotka–Volterra type symmetric competition. The emergence of a coexistence regime here rests in part on specific properties of the Moran modelling framework, in particular its fixed overall population size that enforces instant re-colonization after death events, as well as on the (positive) mortality and resuscitation rates of the dormant state. We provide heuristic explanations for the observed types of behaviour and the corresponding proofs, which involve comparisons to suitable branching processes, approximations by dynamical systems, and an analysis of asymptotic behaviour of the latter. 

MSC 2010. 92D25, 60J85, 34D05. 

Keywords and phrases. Moran model, selection, dormancy, invasion, coexistence, founder control. 1. Introduction 

Dormancy is an ubiquitous trait that allows organisms to switch into reversible and non-proliferative states that are protected from unfavourable conditions [LdHWB21]. It is generally believed that this strategy, although typically being costly, can contribute to the survival and resilience of species, and may also foster coexistence and diversity in species communities. However, the precise consequences of dormancy in given scenarios are highly sensitive to the modelling of the dormancy-related mechanisms.

For example, in population genetics , which usually assumes fixed (but large) population sizes and long time-scales, it has been shown that dormancy can affect ancestral relationships in multiple and idiosyncratic ways, leading to novel coalescent structures [KKL01, BGKW16], [BGKW18], depending on the involved time-scales and switching mechanisms (e.g. responsive vs stochastic). Moreover, in (stochastic) adaptive dynamics , where population sizes are regulated by Lotka–Volterra type competition on the ecological scale, it has been shown that so-called competition-induced dor-mancy , even when combined with a reduced reproductive rate in order to incorporate the costs of such a strategy, can successfully invade and subsequently fixate in a resident populations without this trait [BT20]. A similar (costly) invasion is not possible for spontaneous dormancy, see e.g. in [P24], again highlighting the relevance of the precise dormancy strategy. This seems to be even more the case in random environments, where several different kinds of dormancy can become optimal, depending on the distributional properties of the environment [BHS21]. Recently, probabilistic research is increasingly bridging the realms of both evolutionary and ecological modelling frameworks – note for example the identification of ancestral structures from population genetics in logistic systems [AFS25, F25, GN25], or the application of coalescent theory in models from stochastic adaptive dynamics on a suitable time-scale [CH24]. In the present paper, we follow these research threads by transferring the idea of competition-induced dormancy into the population-genetic framework of a fixed-size Moran model, more precisely a variant of Moran’s original model [M58] without mutation and with some additional transitions, all considered on the ecological scale. Here, competitive edges become selective advantages, and killed individuals are instantaneously replaced by new offspring. While one would at first glance expect a qualitatively similar behaviour to the one described for a corresponding competitive birth–death model in [BT20] (invasion and subsequent fixation of the dormancy trait), it turns out that new effects, resting crucially on the specifics of the Moran model framework, can appear. In particular, invasion can now lead to 

coexistence of both types instead of fixation of the invader. The corresponding parameter regimes for all possible outcomes, including also so-called founder control (a term borrowed from spatial ecology, see e.g. [V15], referring to fixation of the resident and extinction of the mutant, regardless of their types), can be identified explicitly. This yields an example for a scenario in which dormancy indeed fosters coexistence, in line with general ecological predictions. The organization of the paper is as follows. In Section 2, we define our model and state our main results. In particular, in Section 2.1 we introduce our Moran model, which consists of two types of individuals: One having a selective advantage and the other being able to avoid selective pressure by switching to a dormant state. After some preliminary results on the underlying dynamical system in Section 2.2 and on the branching processes approximating small sub-populations in Section 2.3, in Section 2.4, we present our main results, in particular Theorem 2.8, which determines the fate of a single mutant individual of the dormancy type appearing in a large resident population of the other type. As part of this theorem, we also compute the asymptotic probability of a successful mutant invasion in the large-population limit, as well as the asymptotic time until reaching a macroscopic population size. We further characterize whether a successful invasion leads to coexistence vs a complete fixation of mutants and extinction of residents. In Section 3 we discuss our modelling choices as well as relations to previous work, and we interpret the conditions for invasion and coexistence from the main theorem. Finally, after the statement and proof of some preliminary results on the global stability properties of the underlying two-dimensional dynamical system in Section 4, we carry out the proof of our main result in Section 5. (3) When a type 1 and a type 2a individual meet, we assume that type 1 has a selective advantage that amounts to being more likely to be chosen as the reproducing individual. Three phases of an invasion and two branching processes. We will study the situation where at time zero, the initial condition of our Moran model is X(N )(0) = ( N − 1, 1, 0) , i.e. almost all individuals are from the resident type 1 and there is one single active type 2 “mutant” individual. Our main result below, Theorem 2.8, determines the fate of the population after the appearance of this first mutant individual for large N . To be able to state and prove this theorem, we need to define the (asymptotic) survival probability of an invader, and its invasion fitness . Both quantities can be derived from a suitable coupled branching process for the invader during the early phase of the invasion. Hence, we already here discuss the three classical phases of the invasion, similarly to the strategy in the seminal paper of Champagnat [C06], where a competitive Lotka–Volterra type model with logistic competition was studied. 

• Phase I: As long as the mutant population is small compared to N (but not extinct), the type 1 population size remains close to N thanks to the constant population size assumption. Hence, the influence of the resident on the invader is almost constant, and we can approximate the mutant population size by an autonomous two-type binary branching process.

In this section, we begin with a review our modelling assumptions in Section 3.1, discuss the partic-ular consequences of the fixed population size assumption of the Moran model in Section 3.2, review some relations to existing work in Section 3.3, interpret the mutual invasion conditions in Section 3.4 and finally comment on the metastability of coexistence equilibria for stochastic systems in Section 3.5. 3.1. Modelling choices. In our Moran model, reproduction is incorporated by picking pairs of par-ticles at a fixed rate, and choosing one of them for (potential) death and the other one for potential reproduction. This is formally similar to modelling competition events in the approximation of Lotka– Volterra models (as in [FM04]), where, however, only one particle dies, and reproduction is completely disentagled from competitive events. We include a selective advantage of one type with the help of our parameter s, which gives a higher chance to the type 1 particle to become the reproducing parent. Note that the fixed population size assumption of the Moran model here enforces the simultaneity of competitive death and reproduction. One novelty in this Moran set-up is that particles exhibiting a dormancy trait can avoid competitive death and thus compensate selective disadvantages in a particular way, similar to the Lotka–Volterra set-up in [BT20]. We deem it natural to allow escape into dormancy both for intra- and inter-species competition (with the same probability). A similar entanglement appears in the dormant state for strictly positive death rates κ. Here, if a dormant particle dies, we assume that its spot is immediately taken by a uniformly chosen particle from THE INTERPLAY OF SELECTION AND DORMANCY IN A MORAN MODEL 13 

the whole (currently present) population. Since we do not consider this a competitive/selective event, individuals of all types are equally likely to recolonize. Again, this instant recolonization is due to the fixed population size assumption of the Moran. Interestingly, this is also precisely the mechanism that allows for the emergence of coexistence (for suitable parameters), in contrast to the Lotka–Volterra type model in [BT20], where dormant individuals died at a linear rate without replacement and coexistence (or founder control) was impossible. See the discussion in Section 3.2 below. In the light of the above, two modification of the dynamics of the model seem to be plausible, although we refrain from investigating their full consequences here: 

• One could introduce a selective advantage according to s also when re-colonizing vacant slot due to death in the dormant state. 

• One could pick different different transition probabilities, say p1, p 2a, for escape into dormancy depending on the type of the potential parent ( 1 or 2a), and even p2a = 0 might be considered. We believe that in both cases, coexistence regimes still exist, but do not delve into the details here. Regarding time-scales, recall that in our model we assume strong selection of order O(1) and weak dormancy times (also O(1) ) on the ecological scale. One might be tempted to switch to an evolu-tionary scale O(N ) with weak selection s/N and strong dormancy times O(N ) and try to identify a corresponding diffusion limit. We leave this approach for future investigation. 3.2. Consequences of the Moran modelling framework. It turns out that the fixed population size assumption of the Moran model has profound consequences, both on the level of results and the technical treatment of the model. These stem from two main effects: First, the death of an individual always occurs simultaneously with birth of another one, so that there are no ‘vacant population slots’ (instant recolonization). Second, the fixed total population size means that in the description of the model one type out of 1, 2a, 2d is always redundant, and the corresponding ODE limits are two- instead of three dimensional. We begin with the consequences of instant recolonization, in particular during the death of a dormant individual. In absence of this, i.e. for κ = 0 , invasion implies fixation: Coexistence and founder control never occur. This was also already true in the Lotka–Volterra type model of [BT20] with symmetric competition and competition-induced dormancy, even in the variant of the model that included death in the dormant state. While for κ = 0 , the proof of Theorem 2.8 is an easy adaptation of that of the main result of [BT20], this is not true anymore for κ > 0 and in that case, the behaviour of the model of the present paper becomes much more complex. The point is that recolonization of slots vacated by type 2 particles always helps the type 1 particles. However, it turns out that the effect of increasing κ is different for small σ than for large σ. Assume that the choice of the parameters s, p, σ is such that for κ = 0 , type 2 fixates. Then, if σ is small, increasing κ first makes (1 , 0) stable and thus it leads to founder control (lack of invasion of type 2), and only afterwards it makes (0 , ¯x2a) unstable, leading to the fixation of type 1. When σ is large, the stability of these two equilibria changes in the reverse order, and in the intermediate regime where 

(0 , ¯x2a) is already unstable but (1 , 0) is still also unstable, we have coexistence (invasion of type 1). Eventually, for κ large enough, type 1 will again reach fixation. The lower dimensionality, in particular for the many-particle ODE limit, has technical advantages, allowing for a more complete analysis of asymptotic behaviour involving elements of the Poincaré– Bendixson theory, in particular the Bendixson–Dulac criterion. However, on a technical level, com-putations related to the existence and stability of equilibria of the dynamical system are often still substantially more involved than in the case of the dynamical system corresponding to [BT20], despite the fact that the latter system is rank-3 three-dimensional. 14 JOCHEN BLATH, BAPTISTE LE DUIGOU AND ANDRÁS TÓBIÁS 

3.3. Relation to existing models. The dichotomy between a coexistence regime and a founder control regime has been observed in many different types of population-dynamic models before. One of the most classical population-dynamic models is the two-type competitive Lotka–Volterra model. Here, when competition is symmetric between the two types, invasion implies fixation (namely, the type with the higher net reproduction rate wins). But if one makes competition asymmetric , depending on the strength of intra- vs. interspecific competition, a regime of stable coexistence or one of founder control emerges. In the regime of stable coexistence, the system has a stable coordinate-wise positive equilibrium, while in the case of founder control, it has an unstable one, and in the case of fixation of one type it has no coordinate-wise positive equilibrium. See e.g. [B83] for a reference. In these Lotka–Volterra models, coexistence is equivalent to the coordinate-wise nonzero equilib-rium having two positive coordinates and being stable, which also both have to be smaller than the corresponding positive coordinates of the equilibria with exactly one positive and one zero coordinate. The characterization of founder control is similar, but here the coordinate-wise positive equilibrium is unstable. This is also similar to our model because here, for σ > 2s2 

> p(1 −s)

, type 1 can invade if and only if ˜x2a < ¯x2a and type 2 can invade if and only if ˜x2a > 0, i.e. ˜x1 < 1. I.e., intuitively speaking, type 1 can invade if and only if it can reduce the type 2a population size at equilibrium, and type 2 can invade if and only if it can reduce the type 1 population size at equilibrium. We do not know whether 

(˜ x1, ˜x2a) is stable in the coexistence regime and unstable in the founder control regime, but we expect that this is the case (cf. Conjecture 4.5). A microbial phenomenon with a similar but even stronger effect than asymmetric competition is horizontal gene transfer. There have been different ways of modelling it in the mathematical litera-ture, see e.g. [BCFMT16, BCFMT18, CMT19, BPT23], but the way of modelling most similar to the instantaneous recolonization in the present paper is the one in [BT21]. Indeed, here any individual of one type is able to turn individuals of the other type into its own type, at a rate proportional to the population size of the other type. Thanks to this feature, under certain conditions even an unfit type that would be able to survive on its own can coexist with the other type (even if the latter is capable of competition-induced dormancy modelled similarly to [BT20]), which is not the case for usual asymmetric competition. Apart from the regimes of fixation and coexistence, for different choices of the parameters, a founder control regime was also identified in [BT21]. 3.4. Interpretation of the invasion conditions. 3.5. Stable vs metastable coexistence. An ubiquitous and well-known caveat that arises when sto-chastic individual based systems are approximated by ODEs is that stable equilibria in the dynamical system typically do not imply perpetual stability in the stochastic system, due to random fluctuations, even in the Moran model set-up, where the overall population size remains constant for all times. In particular, in our set-up, even if one can verify that under the coexistence scenario (iii) the system (2.1) converges to (˜ x1, ˜x2a) as time tends to infinity, this does not mean simultaneous perpetual non-extinction of types 1 and 2 in the Moran model. Indeed, the Markov chain (X(N )(t)) t≥0 will almost surely either reach the absorbing state (N, 0, 0) or the subset {(0 , x 2a, N −x2a) : x2a ∈ { 0, 1, . . . , N }} of the state space and stay there forever (restricted to the latter subset, the Markov chain is irreducible, and hence almost surely neither type 2a nor type 2d can go extinct forever). Thus, either type 1 or type 2 will die out in almost surely finite time for fixed N , leading to the fixation of the other type. Let us note that in models with logistic competition, not only coexistence is merely metastable, but also the entire population dies out in almost surely finite time (in contrast to the Moran model set-up). However, the expected extinction time grows exponentially with the carrying capacity. 4.
